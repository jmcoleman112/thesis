\section{Background Literature Review}

To achieve the aims laid out in the Introduction, a background literature review has been performed to ensure that the methodology described later utilises best modern practices and research, and targets a clear gap in research. Focus will be placed on an overview of the problem, common approaches and prevalent models, common model compression techniques, and common full-stack system techniques. 

\subsection{Literature Review: Overview} As discussed in the introduction, modern sporting preparation and broadcast have put a larger emphasis on quantitative data than opinion-based analysis, especially with the rise of next-generation tracking and sensor technologies \cite{ReinMemmert2016}. Some of these implementations explore tracking key statistics such as team pass completion percentages, possession, and team formation analysis through the use of object detection/tracking and pitch recognition \cite{zheng2025_review_football_cv,Mooney2014CVSports}. However, emphasis in this niche has been largely placed on accuracy over computational efficiency or real-time analysis, with some designed software systems requiring over 515B Floating Point Operations Per Inference (FLOPs) \cite{roboflow_sports_repo}, which edge devices cannot maintain.

\subsection{Literature Review: Common Approaches}
Modern computer vision-based approaches to soccer analysis often combine object detection, object tracking, and pitch keypoint estimation to generate high-level match statistics \cite{tracking}. Object detection models are used to localise players, goalkeepers, referees, and the ball, while keypoint detection models identify relevant pitch markings such as penalty box corners and the centre circle \cite{IEEE2024PitchKeypoints}. The outputs of these two models are then combined through a post-inference pipeline to produce statistics such as possession, pass completion, and formation maps, as discussed in detail in Section \ref{subsubsec:postinference}.

\subsubsection{Common Approaches: Object Detection}
\label{subsubsec:object}
The object detection portion of the above solutions is commonly addressed using YOLO (You Only Look Once) models \cite{Redmon_2016_CVPR}. YOLO models utilise a single pass for both localisation and detection, resulting in a low-latency detection architecture ideal for real-time analysis. This has been previously explored in soccer analysis, with models producing $\text{mAP}_{50\text{-}95}$ of up to 91.5\% using labelled broadcast soccer frames \cite{footandball}. As discussed earlier, $\text{mAP}_{50\text{-}95}$ represents the model's $\text{mAP}$ averaged across IoU thresholds from 50\% to 95\%. A higher-level analysis of YOLO models will be explored further in Section \ref{lit:yolo}. Another potential real-time detection model is the Faster R-CNN model. Faster R-CNN utilises a 2-stage pipeline, which first separates images into candidate regions, before performing classification on a second pass \cite{7485869}. While used in post-match processing, Faster R-CNN is likely not applicable to real-time scenarios, due to this heavy 2-stage inference process. \subsubsection{Common Approaches: Key-point Detection}
\label{subsubsec:keypoint}

Offline systems often use encoder–decoder architectures, which approximate pitch key locations by compressing image features into a latent representation and then decoding this representation to produce precise key-point predictions \cite{Chu_2022_CVPR}. More recently, YOLO-based pose detection models have been applied \cite{Maji_2022_CVPR}, utilising the single-pass architecture to enable low-latency key-point estimation. Some classical, non-neural approaches also exist, which align lines detected on screen with known pitch dimensions to project player positions into real-world coordinates without learned models \cite{6516867}. While this enables significantly faster throughput and avoids the need for training data, performance is often sensitive to occlusion, inaccurate line detection, and changes in broadcast viewpoint or visual clutter, limiting robustness in real match conditions.
 
\subsubsection{Common Approaches: Post-Inference and Statistic Generation}
\label{subsubsec:postinference}
With player bounding boxes and pitch keypoints established, post-inference processing is often applied to generate high-level match statistics. This typically involves three stages: team assignment, homography estimation, and statistic generation.

\textbf{Team Assignment.} Once players are detected and tracked, they are assigned to one of two teams. This is commonly achieved through colour-based clustering applied to the jersey regions of each detected bounding box. K-means clustering is the most widely used approach, grouping players by the dominant colour of their cropped torso region into two clusters corresponding to the two teams, with goalkeepers often handled as a separate class due to their distinctive kit \cite{ZhengLiu2012ColorRecognition}. Mean shift clustering has also been applied as an alternative, offering automatic cluster count estimation at a higher computational cost \cite{ZhengLiu2012ColorRecognition}. The accuracy of team assignment is highly dependent on image quality and the degree of colour similarity between the two kits, with night games or low-resolution broadcast footage presenting particular challenges.

\textbf{Homography Estimation.} To express player positions in real-world pitch coordinates, a homography transformation is estimated between the on-screen image plane and a canonical top-down pitch representation. This is achieved by matching detected pitch keypoints such as penalty box corners, the centre circle, and midfield line intersections to their known positions on a standard pitch template \cite{dubrofsky2009homography}. The resulting homography matrix can then be applied to any pixel coordinate in the image to obtain the corresponding location on the pitch. The accuracy of this transformation is sensitive to the number and distribution of visible keypoints, and degrades when large portions of the pitch are occluded or outside the camera frame.

\textbf{Statistic Generation.} With player identities, team assignments, and pitch-relative positions established across frames, a range of high-level statistics can be derived from the tracking history. Possession estimation is typically computed as the proportion of frames in which the ball is within a defined proximity threshold of a player from each team \cite{mradovic38_football_analysis_repo,hmzbo_football_analytics_dl_cv_repo}. Pass detection identifies ball ownership transitions between players of the same team, allowing pass completion rates and passing networks to be constructed. Player-level statistics such as distance covered and instantaneous speed can also be derived by integrating the displacement of each tracked identity across consecutive frames in pitch coordinates \cite{mradovic38_football_analysis_repo,hmzbo_football_analytics_dl_cv_repo}.

\subsection{Literature Review: Modern YOLO Architecture}
\label{lit:yolo}

As YOLO models are regularly used for object detection and keypoint localisation, a more detailed review of these is warranted. Recent YOLO detectors, such as the Ultralytics YOLO11 and Ultralytics YOLO26 families, generally follow a backbone-neck-head design \cite{UltralyticsYOLO, unknown}. In this, the backbone extracts image features at increasing levels of abstraction, while the neck combines them so that both fine spatial detail and higher-level semantic information can be used by the detection head. To do so, the backbone utilises a variety of convolutional layers (labelled Conv below) and variations of CSP bottlenecks with 3 convolutions (labelled C3K2 below). The neck uses upsampling, concatenation, and C3K2 blocks, allowing the head to produce predictions at three resolutions, corresponding broadly to small, medium, and large objects \cite{UltralyticsYOLO, unknown}. A simplified version of this architecture is displayed below in Figure~\ref{fig:yolo11_architecture}.


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{images/yolo11.drawio.png}
    \caption{Simplified overview of YOLO11/26 architecture.}
    \label{fig:yolo11_architecture}
\end{figure}


This structure provides an efficient way to scale model size based on user needs. Five variants have been defined (Nano (n), Small (s), Medium (m), Large (l), Extra-Large (x)), which utilise the same architecture, but vary in the depth and width scaling of the C3K2 and Conv blocks, and therefore in parameter count and computational complexity \cite{UltralyticsYOLO, unknown}. One practical and relevant consequence of this architecture is that YOLO models require input dimensions to be exact multiples of 32, due to the cumulative stride of the backbone and neck downsampling stages. This must be accounted for when selecting input resolutions for deployment \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}.

\subsubsection{YOLO11 vs. YOLO26 Architecture}

The YOLO26 family differs from YOLO11 through a series of incremental modifications \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}, instead of through drastic architectural changes. YOLO26 introduces a new shortcut connection within the neck to improve feature flow through the network, and a new attention mechanism in the final C3k2 stage before detection to place greater emphasis on informative spatial features. In the detection pipeline, YOLO11 utilises distribution focal loss for box regression, whereas YOLO26 instead uses direct box regression and a Non-Maximum-Suppression-free framework. YOLO26 also incorporates Progressive Loss Balancing, small target aware labelling, and a more stable optimisation strategy (MuSGD). These changes indicate that YOLO26 is more focused towards stable training and small-object performance than YOLO11, but the high-level architecture remains largely unchanged \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}.

\subsection{Literature Review: Current Limitations}
Despite the rapid progress in computer vision for sports, several significant technical and operational hurdles remain unexplored for real-time edge deployment. As Tables \ref{softwarestacks}, \ref{modelparams}, and \ref{tab:yolo11_pose_tradeoff} displayed earlier, common models used today \cite{hmzbo_football_analytics_dl_cv_repo, yang2025soccermaster, mradovic38_football_analysis_repo, roboflow_sports_repo} are computationally intensive and would require high-end GPU resources (e.g., NVIDIA A100 GPUs) that are typically unavailable on constrained edge devices. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:edge_devices}, even a mid-range candidate such as the Jetson Orin Nano provides only a 1024-core Ampere GPU and 8~GB of RAM, making the computation demanded by common pipelines currently infeasible. Most systems are designed for batch frame processing, requiring 200+ GFLOPS per inference \cite{yang2025soccermaster, roboflow_sports_repo}. Those that may support real-time operation still produce only 5--6 FPS on consumer hardware \cite{hmzbo_football_analytics_dl_cv_repo, mradovic38_football_analysis_repo}. This leads to systems and software unsuitable for edge deployment without first applying optimisation or compression. 



\subsection{Literature Review: Compression Techniques}

As warranted by the Current Limitations, a range of model compression techniques designed to reduce the computational and memory requirements of deep learning models while preserving acceptable analytical performance have been explored in literature. Although these techniques have not yet been widely applied to end-to-end soccer statistic generation systems, they will form a strong foundation for deploying real-time soccer analysis pipelines on edge devices.


\subsubsection{Compression Techniques: Hardware Acceleration}

Hardware acceleration improves inference efficiency by executing neural networks through runtimes and software stacks that are specifically optimised for the target hardware platform \cite{tensorrt}. Rather than relying on a default framework execution path, accelerated backends can apply graph-level optimisations, fuse operations, select more efficient kernels, and better exploit GPU parallelism. This is particularly important in real-time computer vision, where inference latency directly affects whether a system can operate at interactive or live processing speeds.

A common approach to hardware acceleration in embedded and GPU-based deep learning systems is the use of optimised inference runtimes such as TensorRT \cite{tensorrt}. These frameworks convert trained models into deployment-ready checkpoints that are tailored to the hardware on which inference will be performed. These approaches often reduce runtime overhead and improve throughput, making them especially valuable in edge settings where compute and power budgets are constrained. Figure~\ref{fig:hardware_acceleration_lit} illustrates the typical effect of hardware acceleration on inference execution time \cite{tensorrt}.

\subsubsection{Compression Techniques: Quantisation}

Quantisation is one of the most widely adopted compression techniques, and involves reducing the numerical precision of model parameters and activations \cite{inbook}. While standard, uncompressed neural networks typically utilise 32-bit floating-point (FP32) representations, quantisation maps these values to lower-precision formats such as 8-bit (INT8) or 4-bit (INT4) integers.  This process substantially reduces memory bandwidth usage and arithmetic complexity, with INT8 addition being up to 30× more energy-efficient and 116× more area-efficient than its FP32 counterpart on 45nm hardware \cite{inbook}. Figure \ref{fig:quantlit} below displays the effect of different quantisation levels on device power consumption.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images/quantization.png}
    \caption{Effect of Quantisation of Model Energy Consumption \cite{inbook}}
    \label{fig:quantlit}
\end{figure}

As can be seen, identical operations utilise significantly less energy (in Joules) when using less precise values. For example, an 8-bit multiplication requires 0.2 pJ, while a 32-bit multiplication requires 3.1 pJ. This demonstrates the clear optimisation benefit that quantisation can supply.

Two primary approaches to quantisation exist in literature: post-training quantisation and quantisation-aware training \cite{inbook}. Post-training quantisation applies quantisation after training using a small calibration dataset, which avoids heavy re-training and allows the effects of quantisation to be isolated. Quantisation-aware training instead limits precision during training, allowing the model to adapt its weights accordingly. This typically achieves better accuracy retention at the cost of a much longer training process.


\subsubsection{Compression Techniques: Pruning}

The technique of pruning aims to reduce model complexity by removing parameters that contribute minimally to the network’s output \cite{inbook, 10204354}. This technique follows the assumption that the smallest system capable of fitting the training data typically yields the best generalisation on unseen inputs \cite{248452}. It is often performed after training, where a loss function is used to estimate the importance of weights, before identifying and eliminating redundant connections. Following this pruning, a light fine-tuning stage is typically required to recover accuracy lost during the removal process, as the remaining weights must be modified slightly to compensate for the structural changes made to the network.

Pruning strategies are divided into unstructured and structured approaches. Unstructured pruning removes individual weights with low importance regardless of their location \cite{inbook}, which often achieves high compression ratios at the cost of efficient memory manipulation. Structured pruning removes whole structures such as entire filters, channels, or layers, which maintains efficient memory at the cost of less compression. \cite{10204354}. \subsubsection{Compression Techniques: Input Resolution Scaling}
Input resolution scaling reduces computational and memory footprints by lowering the spatial dimensions of the input images processed by a model. Since the complexity of Convolutional Neural Networks and Vision Transformers typically scales quadratically with input resolution, even modest downsizing can yield substantial improvements in inference throughput and energy efficiency \cite{morlier2025input}. Figure \ref{fig:inputreslit} displays model FLOPs and memory usage before and after input resolution scaling is applied.

As can be seen by Figure \ref{fig:inputreslit}, applying input resolution scaling often reduces the required FLOPs by $\sim$2x, with slight improvements in required memory. In the soccer domain, where the ball often occupies fewer than 100 pixels, resolution is a primary determinant of performance; for instance, increasing resolution from 640px to 1200px has been shown to improve ball detection recall by up to 96\% \cite{10756443}. 

\subsubsection{Compression Techniques: Knowledge Distillation}
Knowledge distillation is the final model compression technique to be explored, in which a compact "student" model is trained to replicate the performance of a high-capacity "teacher" model. Unlike standard training, the student is optimised using softened output distributions (logits) or intermediate feature representations produced by the teacher, which contain richer information than hard ground-truth labels \cite{Kaleem2024ACR, inbook}. Knowledge transfer can be categorised into three types \cite{Kaleem2024ACR}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Response-based, which focuses on the final output probabilities of the teacher model.
    \item Feature-based, which aims to mimic the teacher's internal feature maps to capture implicit patterns.
    \item Relation-based, which teaches the student the relationships between different data samples or layers.
\end{itemize}
Two commonly applied feature-based approaches are Channel-Wise Distillation, which normalises teacher and student feature maps channel-wise before computing the distillation loss, and Masked Generative Distillation, which randomly masks regions of the student’s features and trains a generation function to reconstruct the teacher’s full feature maps \cite{Kaleem2024ACR}. Channel-Wise Distillation therefore focuses on direct alignment of feature responses, whereas Masked Generative Distillation encourages the student to infer missing structure and thereby learn richer internal representations.

Note that unlike prior compression techniques, knowledge distillation does not directly reduce the number of parameters or FLOPs of the student model. Instead, it enables a smaller model to achieve performance closer to that of a larger teacher, effectively improving the accuracy-efficiency trade-off by increasing accuracy rather than lowering latency.
