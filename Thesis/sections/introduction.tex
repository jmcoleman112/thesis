\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background and Motivation}
The use of advanced analytical methods in sport gained popularity in the mid-1990s through sabermetric principles in professional baseball \cite{Moneyball}. Since then, data-driven analysis has become central to performance evaluation, tactical decision-making, and modern sports broadcasting. During live coverage, high-level statistics such as possession, shot counts, and pass completion rates are commonly shown alongside the scoreline and elapsed time. Historically, however, the collection of this data has typically been outsourced to specialised and expensive third-party analytics providers. For example, major English and Scottish football leagues have long-term data rights agreements with Opta, granting exclusive responsibility for the collection and distribution of official match statistics across broadcast, media, and analytical platforms \cite{StatsPerform}.

More recently, the growth of online streaming platforms has increased the broadcast coverage of lower-budget and regional sporting events \cite{pwc_sports_streaming_2025}. At the same time, amateur and semi-professional teams are increasingly adopting data-driven approaches to support training and performance evaluation \cite{futuremarketinsights_sports_analytics_2025}. Advances in computer vision now make it possible to analyse sports video streams in real time, enabling low-cost, on-site generation of match statistics without reliance on cloud infrastructure. This could reduce costs for both sports broadcasting and sports science applications, with particular benefits for lower-budget organisations.
\subsection{Current Approaches and Research Gap}
 Much of existing work on this topic has prioritised analytical accuracy and proof-of-concept system design over deployment on constrained hardware. Many proposed pipelines rely on relatively large models, multi-stage processing, or GPU resources that are not realistic for compact edge devices, leaving a gap between strong research prototypes and systems that are practical for low-cost, real-time use in clubs, broadcast settings, or field-side analysis. There is also comparatively limited work examining how optimisation, compression, and end-to-end system design interact in soccer analytics when latency, temperature, power, and GPU/CPU usage must be considered alongside model accuracy. These current approaches and limitations are explored in greater detail in the literature review; at the introduction stage, they motivate the central research gap addressed by this thesis.

\subsection{Problem Aims and Scope}

These trends motivate the aim of this work: \textbf{the optimisation and compression analysis of embedded computer vision models for real-time soccer analysis on an edge device.} For this project, an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano Dev Kit will be used as the target edge device \cite{JetsonOrinNano}. The scope of this project is limited to an in-depth training and compression of a single model family, as well as the development of a single end-to-end system for the extraction of high-level soccer statistics. The specific objectives are as follows: 

\subsubsection{Model Training and System Creation} The first and most basic objective of this project is to train computer-vision models that can automatically extract low-level information from video of soccer games. These models should be trained with the intention of being run on an edge device, but should have an $\text{mAP}_{50}$ within 10\% to that of other modern, non real-time approaches \cite{tracking}.

Alongside these models, an end-to-end system should be developed, to convert the video and model outputs to desired high-level game statistics. This will allow the functionality of the system to be explored, as well as assess hardware and throughput performance of the edge device. 


\subsubsection{Optimization} The second objective is to optimise the end-to-end system with respect to computational efficiency, aiming to reduce CPU, GPU, and memory utilisation, device temperature and power draw without significantly affecting analytical performance. Specifically, the system should perform to within 15\% $\text{mAP}_{50}$ of modern object detectors \cite{tracking} used for soccer analysis, while utilizing no more than 80\% of the device's total CPU, GPU, and RAM availability. Similarly, the power draw and temperature should not exceed 80\% of the device's recommended limits, and maintain roughly 20 FPS to allow for real-time analysis. NVIDIA recommends a maximum CPU and GPU temperature of $85^\circ$ and a max power draw of 25W, and so these will used as the initial recommended limits \cite{JetsonOrinNano}.

\subsubsection{Compression}
The third objective is to apply model-level compression techniques directly to the trained models. As with before, compressed models should maintain an analytical performance within 15\% $\text{mAP}_{50}$ of the baseline uncompressed models, while targetting a FLOP (Floating Point Operations Per Second) reduction of 5x and a parameter size reduction of 5x. This will also indirectly lower GPU/CPU and power requirements, supporting the 80\% total CPU, GPU, and RAM availability aims discussed earlier.
