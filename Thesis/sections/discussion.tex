\section{Discussions and Conclusions}

\subsection{Feasibility of Embedded Soccer Analysis}

The results above display multiple key insights regarding the topics of this thesis. The first and most direct is that real-time soccer analysis on embedded hardware like the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano is achievable. Figures \ref{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency} and \ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} indicate that these systems can attain comparable accuracies to uncompressed models, while achieving significant reductions in latency and FPS near 12--13. This is a promising result, suggesting that edge-based sports analytics can be practical for real-world applications, and leads to the conclusion that a full-stack system of this kind can operate below \textbf{80\%} total GPU utilisation without exceeding \textbf{80\%} of the device's temperature and power limits.

\subsection{Compression Technique Effectiveness}

A second key insight concerns the varying effectiveness of different compression techniques depending on the task and model size. Across all model tasks and sizes, hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation provided significant latency reductions with minimal accuracy loss, making them the most broadly effective techniques. Input resolution scaling produced extremely high latency reductions with little accuracy loss for some models (YOLO26n-pose and YOLO11n-pose), but was less effective for larger pose models. Similarly, small input reductions (75\%) proved beneficial for smaller object detection models, but large input reductions or large object models often caused significant accuracy drops. Pruning was more effective for object detection than for pose estimation, and pruning beyond 90\% seemed far too aggressive for most keypoint estimation models. Finally, knowledge distillation offered very small latency reductions but maintained accuracy well, suggesting it is most useful as a support technique to preserve accuracy when applying more aggressive compression, rather than as a direct speed-up method on its own. Taken together, these findings support the conclusion that object detection and pitch keypoint estimation models can be effectively compressed to 15\% of their original latency while remaining within 15\% mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ of their uncompressed counterparts.


\subsection{Full-Stack System FPS}
A further important point of discussion is the throughput performance of the full-stack system. The final implementation operated at approximately 12--13 FPS, which falls short of the 20 FPS target for real-time performance. However, this result should be interpreted in the context of prior work. As discussed earlier, previous systems were not designed for real-time deployment and, in preliminary benchmarking on the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano, achieved fewer than 1 FPS. Similarly, early and heavily unoptimised versions of the system developed in this thesis achieved only around 3 FPS on the same device \cite{my_conference_paper}. Figure \ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} further shows that the final system achieves an approximately 2.2x reduction in latency when compressed models are used in place of uncompressed ones. These results indicate that the optimisation techniques applied in this thesis represent substantial progress towards real-time embedded soccer analysis, even if the 20 FPS target was not fully achieved. The target remains likely reachable through more systematic system-level optimisations, such as more efficient data loading and pre-processing pipelines, and further model compression.
