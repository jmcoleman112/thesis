\section{Discussion and Conclusions}
\label{sec:discussion}

With the results of the model training, compression, and full-stack system evaluation thoroughly presented, a higher-level discussion of the findings can be held. The main themes to be focussed on are the feasibility of embedded soccer analysis and the effectiveness/best practices of model compression in achieving this. The key limitations of the work are then discussed in order to contextualise these findings.


\subsection{Feasibility of Embedded Soccer Analysis}

\textbf{The main conclusion of this work is that when compression techniques are applied effectively, a full-stack soccer analysis system can be deployed on a low-cost edge device in a manner useful for real-world applications.} However, achieving the ideal target throughput of 20~FPS will require further system-level engineering or model-training optimisations, rather than additional model compression. Figures \ref{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency} and \ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} support the first part of this conclusion most directly, indicating that the compressed full-stack systems can operate within 15\% of the relevant baselines while reaching a throughput of 12--13~FPS and an end-to-end latency of 90.6~ms. This represents a substantial improvement over both the uncompressed configuration, which achieved fewer than 3~FPS on the same hardware during early development \cite{my_conference_paper}, as well as an improvement over prior implementations in the literature, several of which had no real-time optimisation at all. From a hardware resource perspective, the deployed system operated well within the constraints defined in the problem aims; GPU utilisation averaged 40--55\%, power draw 5--15~W, and device temperature 48--56\textdegree C against respective limits of 80\%, 20~W, and 68\textdegree C. This indicates that the system is not at risk of exceeding recommended power or thermal limits.

\textbf{The second aspect of this conclusion is that further throughput improvements will likely require system-level optimisations rather than additional model compression.} While the compressed system achieved the minimum target of 100~ms latency (10~FPS) defined in the problem aims, the ideal standard of 20~FPS for real-time analysis was not met. Figures \ref{fig:best_model_analysis_object_hardware_grid} and \ref{fig:best_model_analysis_pose_hardware_grid} indicate that object detection and keypoint localisation each reach latency plateaus of around 15--30~ms regardless of mAP$_{50\text{-}95}$, and therefore further compression of the individual models is unlikely to yield meaningful throughput improvements. This suggests that the bottleneck for these models is no longer arithmetic operation, but instead data movement and overhead. At larger model scales, inference time seems dominated by arithmetic operations that are affected by model compression and hardware optimisation. At smaller scales, this arithmetic cost may be reduced to the point where the fixed overheads of data transfers, kernel latency, and controller bandwidth begin to dominate. This interpretation is supported by the GPU utilisation results, which show only 40--55\% usage for the shortlisted models, indicating that data movement is the limiting factor at these scales, rather than computation. Instead, optimisations to the full stack system, including parallelisation and modified inference intervals, are likely to be the most effective pathways to reducing latency. Further, the Jetson Orin Nano is a relatively low-end edge device, and the use of more powerful hardware such as the Orin NX or Orin AGX would likely yield significant throughput improvements without requiring major changes to the trained models or overall system design.

\subsection{Compression Technique and Model Selection}

Beyond the feasibility of soccer analysis, a key discussion point in this work is the effectiveness of both model choice and compression selection. This can be separated into three main points: the effect of model task on compression, the choice of YOLO11 versus YOLO26, and the effect of model scale on compression.

\subsubsection{Effect of Model Task on Compression}
\label{task_compression_discussion}

A consistent theme across the results is that object detection and keypoint estimation respond to compression in different ways, and this separation is the most important factor to consider when selecting compression techniques for a given deployment. 

\textbf{Hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation were robust across both tasks, delivering substantial latency reductions with negligible accuracy cost regardless of model family or scale.} These two techniques therefore represent a safe first step for any compression pipeline, independent of task. \textbf{However, aggressive compression was consistently less suitable for keypoint estimation then object detection}. INT8 quantisation was viable for object detection at medium-to-large model scales, but was damaging for keypoint estimation for nearly all variants. Similarly, input resolution was consistently less useful for keypoint estimation than for object detection. Structured pruning followed a similar pattern: object detection models generally tolerated moderate pruning with recoverable fine-tuning, while keypoint estimation models were more fragile, particularly under aggressive retention targets.

The most likely reason is the nature of the prediction task itself. Object detection mainly requires coarser bounding-box localisation, and is therefore often more tolerant of reduced precision, lower input resolution, and moderate channel removal. Keypoint estimation, by contrast, depends on very accurate spatial localisation of multiple keypoints, making it more sensitive to any compression method that reduces fine-detailed feature representations. This helps explain why keypoint models in this study were generally less robust to INT8 quantisation, input resolution reduction, and structured pruning.


\subsubsection{Effect of Model Family on Compression}

As discussed in the literature review, the YOLO11 and YOLO26 families are architecturally similar, with their main differences lying in their loss functions and label-assignment strategies. Therefore, the results indicating broadly similar behaviour across the training and compression experiments is not unexpected. However, meaningful differences still emerged in specific scenarios, which enable a clearer assessment of which family is better suited to soccer object detection and pitch keypoint localisation.


For object detection, the uncompressed YOLO26 models consistently achieved marginally higher mAP\textsubscript{50\text{-}95} than YOLO11 at equivalent medium-to-large scales, with YOLO26l and YOLO26x reaching \textbf{0.928} and \textbf{0.929} respectively against \textbf{0.907} and \textbf{0.896} for their YOLO11 counterparts. At the smaller scales this relationship changes, with YOLO11n achieving \textbf{0.857} against \textbf{0.842} for YOLO26n. This suggests that the benefits of YOLO26 are less pronounced when parameter counts are limited. For keypoint estimation, results were broadly comparable between families at medium and large scales, with the exception of YOLO26n-pose, which heavily outperformed YOLO11n-pose at \textbf{0.993} against \textbf{0.923} mAP\textsubscript{50-95} with similar latency. A potential explanation is YOLO26's small-target labelling changes and introduction of attention, which should improve the detection of small objects such as pitch keypoints \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}. At larger model scales, both YOLO11 and YOLO26's parameter counts appear to provide sufficient capacity for accurate pitch keypoint localisation, regardless of variations in training strategies. At the nano scale, however, the YOLO26 small-target-aware training strategy may offer a larger benefit, as the model must operate using a much smaller set of feature channels. This may also help explain why YOLO26n-pose was more robust to input resolution scaling: training that emphasises small features may better preserve keypoint localisation when image detail is reduced.

For the most part, the effect of compression was similar across YOLO11 and YOLO26 models, with both families responding similarly to hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation. The clearest compression based difference can be seen with pose-model pruning: YOLO11-pose pruning was achievable across most sizes with recoverable accuracy, while YOLO26-pose pruning produced training instability across most models and never truly offered comparable results. One potential cause is the more complex inter-layer connectivity introduced by YOLO26's attention mechanism and modified neck shortcut connections \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}. When structured pruning removes channels involved in the attention gate, the remaining network must simultaneously recover both the detection and attention connections during fine-tuning. This is likely a more complex task than the channel recovery required in YOLO11's topology, where feature dependencies between successive layers are less tightly coupled without the heavy use of attention. This is also likely compounded by the earlier conclusion that keypoint estimation models in general are more sensitive to pruning, as the loss of key feature channels is more damaging to the fine-grained spatial understanding required for accurate keypoint localisation.

\textbf{Overall, YOLO26 is generally the preferable family, offering slightly stronger analytical performance for object detection at medium-to-large scales and a substantial accuracy advantage at the nano scale for pose estimation, both in compressed and uncompressed variants.} As discussed, the only concern to consider is pruning: YOLO26-pose exhibited consistent training instability under structured pruning, and deployments requiring aggressive pruning of keypoint models should favour YOLO11-pose instead.

\subsubsection{Effect of Model Size on Compression}

The next key discussion is the effect of model scale on compression. Before compression, object detection accuracy increased regularly through Nano, Small, and Medium sizes, with negligible improvements beyond that point. This immediately indicates that the Large and X-Large models are likely unnecessarily big and overly-parameterised for this task. Keypoint estimation accuracy was consistently high across all scales, with most uncompressed variants exceeding \textbf{0.97} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$, initially suggesting that the nano scale would be the most suitable. 

As expected, the results indicate that bigger models compress more effectively. FP16 quantisation and hardware acceleration remained robust at all sizes, though the latency benefit of acceleration scaled positively with model size: YOLO11x achieved a \textbf{36.0\%} latency reduction versus only \textbf{14.5\%} for YOLO11n. INT8 quantisation was also more damaging at smaller scales, likely as reduced parameter counts left less redundant complexity to absorb the precision loss. Input resolution reduction produced similar absolute accuracy losses across scales, with smaller models finding less latency improvements. Structured pruning was similarly size-dependent, with larger models tolerating harsher prunings before accuracy degraded, with only light pruning remaining viable for some smaller models.

\textbf{Collectively, the results indicate that the compact baseline size of the Nano and Small models makes them more effective in terms of latency, despite the larger models' greater compatibility with some compression methods.} While aggressive pruning, quantisation, and resolution reduction can yield larger absolute gains on larger models, lighter techniques such as FP16 quantisation, 75\% input reduction, and light pruning are effective enough to give the Nano and Small models more favourable latency--accuracy trade-offs than their larger counterparts. This conclusion suggests that these tasks do not offer sufficient visual diversity to fully utilise the complexity of medium-to-large models, indicating that the larger models are overly-parameterised. When a model carries substantial unused capacity, aggressive compression may remove redundant structure without proportional accuracy loss. Conversely, a Nano model already operating near the minimum capacity required for this task has less redundancy, which might explain why INT8 quantisation and heavy pruning damage smaller models so severely. For work targeting higher-complexity data, such as pitchside or wide-angle footage with greater viewpoint variation, larger models may prove more competitive after compression, as additional task complexity would better utilise their representational advantage before parameters are removed.

\subsubsection{Best Compression Technique and Model Strategy}

These family and size discussions can be combined to determine the best strategy for real-time soccer analysis on an edge device. As established above, YOLO26 is the preferred family for keypoint localisation, while either family remains viable for object detection, and Nano/Small scale models offer the most favourable accuracy--latency trade-offs for both tasks. The best compression technique can also be deduced from the above discussion. FP16 quantisation and moderate input resolution reduction should be applied as a first step, as both consistently deliver substantial latency improvements with negligible accuracy cost across all model scales and families. Aggressive techniques (pruning below 80\% retention, INT8 quantisation, and input resolution below 75\% of the baseline) should generally be avoided for keypoint localisation, given the high accuracy penalties observed. However, light pruning and knowledge distillation may prove beneficial. The last conclusion is that additional compression of individual models is unlikely to yield meaningful throughput improvements, as both tasks approach latency plateaus of 15--30~ms per model. The likely best pathway to achieving the ideal 20~FPS target therefore lies in system-level optimisations, rather than additional model compression. As mentioned earlier, this represents an area for future research beyond the scope of this work.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several methodological limitations should be noted to qualify the findings presented in this thesis and set up analysis for future work, although none fundamentally undermine the core conclusions.

The first is the use of a single object detection dataset for the primary compression study. While the Football Analytics dataset was selected based on superior initial performance across all model scales, its broadcast-footage origin means that performance on pitchside or wide-angle camera footage remains untested. That said, the consistency of compression trends across all model families and scales suggests these findings are likely to generalise across different datasets.

The second limitation is the restriction to a single edge device, the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. While this device is widely used and represents a reasonable price point, absolute latency and hardware-utilisation values are hardware-specific and cannot be directly transferred to other platforms. The broad behaviour of the compression techniques may still be informative for similar GPU-based edge devices, but the magnitude of the gains, particularly for hardware acceleration and deployment latency, should be expected to vary across hardware platforms.

The final limitation is the use of a single full-stack system architecture for high-level evaluation. Alternative system designs, such as a dedicated ball localisation model or non-model-based keypoint detection, may yield different and higher accuracy-efficiency trade-offs and represent clear directions for future work. The normalised confusion matrix displayed in Figure \ref{fig:confusion_matrix} indicates that a major source of error in the object detection models was the misclassification of the ball, which suggests that a dedicated ball detection model could reduce the burden on the player detector. This narrowing of model task could then allow both detection models to be compressed much more aggressively. This could then in turn produce both a more accurate and faster full-stack system.
