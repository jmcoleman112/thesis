\section{Discussion and Conclusions}

With the results of the model training, compression, and full-stack system evaluation thoroughly presented, this chapter provides a higher-level discussion of the findings. The main themes are the feasibility of embedded soccer analysis, the effectiveness and best practices of model compression, and the performance of the proposed full-stack system. These findings are then qualified through a discussion of the key limitations of the work.


\subsection{Feasibility of Embedded Soccer Analysis}

The main conclusion of this work is that, when compression techniques are applied effectively, a full-stack soccer analysis system can be deployed on a low-cost edge device such as the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano in a manner useful for real-world applications. However, achieving the ideal target throughput of 20~FPS will require further system-level engineering optimisations or different model-training choices, rather than additional model compression. Figures \ref{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency} and \ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} support this conclusion most directly, indicating that the compressed full-stack systems can operate within 15\% of the relevant baselines while reaching a throughput of 12--13~FPS and an end-to-end latency of 90.6~ms. This represents a substantial improvement over both the uncompressed configuration, which achieved fewer than 3~FPS on the same hardware during early development, and prior implementations in the literature, several of which produced fewer than 1~FPS on equivalent hardware. From a hardware resource perspective, the deployed system operated well within the constraints defined in Section~3.3.2. GPU utilisation averaged \textbf{40--55\%}, power draw \textbf{5--15~W}, and device temperature \textbf{48--56\textdegree C}, against respective limits of 80\%, 20~W, and 68\textdegree C. This indicates that the system is not at risk of exceeding safe power or thermal limits.

As mentioned earlier, the main remaining barrier to full feasibility is throughput. While the compressed system achieved the minimum goal of 100~ms latency (10~FPS), the ideal target of 20~FPS was not met. Figures \ref{fig:best_model_analysis_object_hardware_grid} and \ref{fig:best_model_analysis_pose_hardware_grid} indicate that object detection and keypoint localisation each approach latency plateaus of around 15--30~ms, and therefore further compression of the individual models is unlikely to yield meaningful throughput improvements. This plateau is consistent with a transition in the binding constraint: at larger model scales, inference time is dominated by arithmetic operations that are reducible through model compression and hardware optimisation. At nano-to-small scales, this arithmetic cost diminishes to the point where fixed overheads --- including memory data transfers, kernel launch latency, and pipeline coordination --- begin to dominate. This interpretation is supported by the GPU utilisation results, which show only 40--55\% usage for the shortlisted models, indicating that the GPU is not compute-saturated and that data movement rather than arithmetic is the limiting factor at these scales. Instead, full-stack system optimisations, including parallelisation and modified inference intervals, are likely to be the most effective pathway to reducing latency. Further, the Jetson Orin Nano is a relatively low-end edge device, and the use of more powerful hardware such as the Orin NX or Orin AGX would likely yield significant throughput improvements without requiring major changes to the trained models or overall system design.

This confirms that the primary limit to embedded soccer analytics is no longer model accuracy alone, but full system-level design. As Figure~\ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} demonstrates, non-model pipeline components --- multi-object tracking, homography estimation, and team assignment --- collectively account for approximately 21\% of total latency in the compressed configuration, a contribution that model-level compression cannot reduce. This system-level perspective is notably absent from the prior implementations reviewed in Section~2.2 \cite{roboflow_sports_repo,mradovic38_football_analysis_repo,hmzbo_football_analytics_dl_cv_repo,yang2025soccermaster}, which report model-level metrics such as mAP and FLOPS in isolation, without characterising the latency contribution of their surrounding pipeline stages.

\subsection{Compression Technique and Model Selection}

Beyond the feasibility of soccer analysis, a key discussion point in this work is the effectiveness of both model choice and compression selection. This can be separated into three main points: the choice of YOLO11 versus YOLO26, the effect of model scale on compression, and the best model and compression pathway for this task.

\subsubsection{Effect of Model Family on Compression}

As discussed in the literature review, the YOLO11 and YOLO26 families are similar in architecture, and differ primarily in their loss functions and label-assignment strategies. Therefore, the broadly similar behaviour of the two families across the training and compression experiments is not unexpected, but the differences remain useful in determining which family is better suited to this task.


For object detection, the uncompressed YOLO26 models consistently achieved marginally higher mAP\textsubscript{50--95} than YOLO11 at equivalent medium-to-large scales, with YOLO26l and YOLO26x reaching 0.928 and 0.929 respectively against 0.907 and 0.896 for their YOLO11 counterparts. At the nano scale, this relationship inverts, with YOLO11n achieving 0.857 against 0.842 for YOLO26n, suggesting that the benefits of YOLO26 are less pronounced when parameter budgets are limited. For keypoint estimation, results were broadly comparable between families at medium and large scales, with the notable exception of YOLO26n-pose, which substantially outperformed YOLO11n-pose at 0.993 against 0.923 mAP\textsubscript{50--95} with similar latency. A plausible architectural explanation is YOLO26's Small-Target-Aware Label Assignment and its attention mechanism in the final neck stage \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}. At larger model scales, representational capacity is sufficient to learn precise pitch marking localisation regardless of the training strategy. At the nano scale, however, this targeted training signal likely provides a proportionally larger benefit: the model must localise geometrically similar pitch features --- corner arcs, penalty box edges, and centre circle segments --- from a compact set of feature channels, and the attention gate may allow it to selectively focus on the most spatially discriminative patterns. This also offers a partial explanation for YOLO26n-pose's greater robustness to resolution reduction observed in the results: a model trained with a small-target-aware signal may more readily recover fine keypoint predictions from degraded inputs than one trained without this bias.

For the most part, the effect of compression was similar across YOLO11 and YOLO26 models. Both families responded similarly to hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation. Under INT8 quantisation, YOLO26 exhibited slightly greater accuracy retention at medium and large object detection scales, consistent with its improved training signal producing weight distributions more amenable to aggressive precision reduction. The most pronounced divergence arose under pose-model pruning: YOLO11-pose pruning was achievable across most scale combinations with recoverable accuracy following fine-tuning, whereas YOLO26-pose pruning produced training instability across multiple configurations and never offered comparable results. A probable cause is the more complex inter-layer connectivity introduced by YOLO26's attention mechanism and modified neck shortcut connections \cite{UltralyticsYOLO}: when structured pruning removes channels contributing to the attention gate, the remaining network must simultaneously recover both the detection and attention objectives during fine-tuning. This is a considerably harder task than the channel recovery required in YOLO11's standard bottleneck topology, where feature dependencies between successive layers are less tightly coupled.

Overall, YOLO26 is generally the preferable family across both tasks and scales, offering marginally stronger analytical performance for object detection at medium-to-large scales and a substantial accuracy advantage at the nano scale for pose estimation. The primary caveat is pruning: YOLO26-pose exhibited consistent training instability under structured pruning, and deployments requiring aggressive pruning of pose estimation models should favour YOLO11-pose instead.

The consistently greater sensitivity of keypoint localisation to aggressive compression --- spanning resolution reduction, INT8 quantisation, and pruning --- reflects both the precision requirements of the task and the downstream consequences of keypoint errors. Object detection is evaluated by IoU overlap, and a prediction displaced by several pixels typically still passes the 0.50 threshold. Keypoint localisation under OKS is inherently more demanding: at thresholds approaching 0.95, sub-pixel accuracy is required at the spatial scales of the pitch markings used here. Beyond the metric itself, keypoint predictions serve as inputs to homography estimation, where localisation errors compound non-linearly: a small displacement in a corner keypoint can translate to a positional error of several metres in the resulting top-down pitch projection. This downstream amplification means that compression-induced degradation in keypoint accuracy carries a disproportionate cost relative to equivalent degradation in object detection, and provides a principled justification for the more conservative compression strategy recommended for pose models.

\subsubsection{Effect of Model Size on Compression}

Another key question to consider is the effect of model scale on compression. Before compression, object detection accuracy increased regularly from nano to medium scale, with diminishing returns beyond that point, immediately indicating that the L and X models are over-parameterised for this task. Pose estimation accuracy was consistently high across all scales, with most uncompressed variants exceeding 0.97 mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$, initially suggesting that the nano scale would be the most suitable. However, this discussion should also account for compression, as larger models may be more suited to compression than smaller ones.

Under compression, model size and technique produced varying degrees of impact. Hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation were robust across all scales, though the latency benefit of acceleration scaled positively with model size, with YOLO11x seeing a 36.0\% latency reduction versus only 14.5\% for YOLO11n. INT8 quantisation was more damaging at smaller scales for object detection, where models possess less numerical redundancy, and was severely damaging for pose estimation at nearly all scales. Input resolution reduction produced broadly comparable relative degradation across object detection scales, but was much more damaging for pose estimation overall, particularly for the larger keypoint models; YOLO26n-pose was the main exception. Light 90\% pruning was more viable at larger scales, where greater representational redundancy exists, although the technique remained viable for some smaller models.

Overall, the results indicate that the compact baseline size of the nano and small models makes them more effective in terms of latency, despite the larger models' greater compatibility with some compression methods. While aggressive pruning, quantisation, and resolution reduction can yield larger absolute gains on larger models, lighter techniques such as FP16 quantisation, 75\% input reduction, and minimal pruning are effective enough to give the N and S models more favourable latency--accuracy trade-offs than their larger counterparts.

This pattern is consistent with the high baseline accuracy achieved across all model scales: object detection models exceeded 0.85 mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ at even the nano scale, and most uncompressed pose models exceeded 0.97. These results suggest that the Football Analytics and pitch keypoint datasets do not present sufficient visual diversity to fully utilise the representational capacity of medium-to-large models, indicating that the larger models are over-parameterised for this task. When a model carries substantial unused capacity, aggressive compression removes redundant structure without proportional accuracy loss. Conversely, a nano model already operating near the minimum capacity required for this task has less redundancy to sacrifice, which explains why INT8 quantisation and heavy pruning disproportionately damage smaller models despite their otherwise favourable latency profiles. For work targeting higher-complexity data --- such as pitchside or wide-angle footage with greater viewpoint variation --- larger models may prove more competitive after compression, as additional task complexity would better utilise their representational advantage before parameters are removed.

\subsubsection{Best Compression Technique and Model Strategy}

These family and size discussions can be combined to determine the best strategy for real-time soccer analysis on an edge device. As established above, YOLO26 is the preferred family for keypoint localisation, while either family remains viable for object detection, and nano-to-small scale models offer the most favourable accuracy--latency trade-offs for both tasks. Building on this, the compression-technique selection follows naturally: FP16 quantisation and moderate input resolution reduction should be applied as a first step, as both consistently deliver substantial latency improvements with negligible accuracy cost across all model scales and families. Aggressive techniques (pruning below 80\% retention, INT8 quantisation, and input resolution below 75\% of the baseline) should generally be avoided for keypoint localisation, given the high accuracy penalties observed. However, INT8 quantisation remains viable for object detection at medium-to-large scales if necessary. A further conclusion is that additional compression of individual models is unlikely to yield meaningful throughput improvements, as both tasks approach latency plateaus of 15--30~ms per model. The primary pathway to achieving the ideal 20~FPS target therefore lies in system-level optimisations rather than additional model compression. As mentioned earlier, this represents a distinction not addressed by prior literature, which has largely focused on model-level optimisation in isolation from system-level constraints.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several methodological limitations should be noted to qualify the findings presented in this thesis, though none fundamentally undermine the core conclusions.

The first is the use of a single object detection dataset for the primary compression study. While the Football Analytics dataset was selected based on superior initial performance across all model scales, its broadcast-footage origin means that performance on pitchside or wide-angle camera footage remains untested. That said, the consistency of compression trends across all model families and scales suggests these findings are likely to generalise across different datasets.

The second limitation is the restriction to a single edge device, the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. While this device is widely used and represents a reasonable price point, absolute latency and hardware-utilisation values are hardware-specific and cannot be directly transferred to other platforms. The broad behaviour of the compression techniques may still be informative for similar GPU-based edge devices, but the magnitude of the gains, particularly for hardware acceleration and deployment latency, should be expected to vary across hardware platforms.

The final limitation is the use of a single full-stack system architecture for high-level evaluation. Alternative system designs, such as a dedicated ball localisation model or non-model-based keypoint detection, may yield different and higher accuracy-efficiency trade-offs and represent clear directions for future work.
