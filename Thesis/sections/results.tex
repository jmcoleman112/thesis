\section{Results}
\subsection{Selecting Model Architecture and Dataset}

As can be seen from Figure \ref{fig:arch_compare}, although Faster R-CNN \cite{Zhao2023RTDETR} and SSD achieved slightly higher initial accuracy, the YOLO family \cite{UltralyticsYOLO} provided substantially lower latency on the Jetson Orin Nano Dev Kit. Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_compare} indicates that the Football Analytics dataset achieved the strongest initial $\mathrm{mAP}_{50\text{--}95}$, at approximately 91\% regardles of the model size, and was therefore selected for the majority of the object detection study.



\subsection{Summary of Model Training}



With the model family and dataset selected, the full training and compression study was then carried out. In total, 904 unique model artefacts were generated and evaluated across the object detection and pitch keypoint tasks. This included 232 \textbf{YOLO11} models, 236 \textbf{YOLO11-pose} models, 204 \textbf{YOLO26} models, and 232 \textbf{YOLO26-pose} models. The majority of this consisted of compressed variants rather than baseline models, reflecting the first central aim of the study: to examine how different compression techniques affect model performance on the edge device. 

\subsection{Compression Analysis Summary}
Before individually exploring each compression technique and desired hardware resource reduction, it is useful to first summarise the overall trends across all trained and compressed models. The following tables and plots provide a high-level overview of how each compression technique (and combination of techniques) affected the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency relationship for the object detection and pitch keypoint models. These summaries allow the general patterns to be identified before a more detailed per-technique analysis is done.

\subsubsection{Compression Summaries: Object Models}
Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} below displays the average change in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ and latency for each of the main optimisation techniques applied to the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object detection models. For concision, the results are averaged across all model sizes (N, S, M, L, X) and both model families (YOLO11 and YOLO26), providing a general sense of the effect of each technique on the object detection task. The effect of model size and family on compression efficiency ignored by this approach will be explored in more detail in the per-technique analysis sections later.

\input{tables/object_technique_summary}

As Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} shows, hardware acceleration is a highly effective technique, reducing latency by \textbf{31.0\%} with a negligible \textbf{-0.3\%} change in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$. FP16 quantisation is also valuable, offering a stronger latency improvement (\textbf{42.8\%}) with a small but slightly larger mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ cost (\textbf{-1.6\%}). INT8 pushes latency lower still (\textbf{52.6\%}), but at a much steeper average cost to accuracy (\textbf{-9.9\%}). Input resolution reduction produces a more incremental trade-off: the 75\% setting offers a strong latency reduction (\textbf{31.5\%}) with a reasonable accuracy cost (\textbf{-3.1\%}), while 60\% and 50\% exhibit increasingly steep trade-offs. Pruning provides only modest latency gains at higher retention levels, with a more meaningful reduction only emerging at the most aggressive setting (\textbf{13.4\%} at 70\%). This high pruning comes with a non-neglible accuracy cost of \textbf{-4.6\%}. 

While Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} indicates that knowledge distillation appears to inflate latency in this summary this is likely an artefact of in-efficent PyTorch-based implementation of knowledge distillation. The true effect of knowledge distillation can be analysed when the models are exported to hardware specific \verb|engine| formats.

These individual results naturally raise the question of how these gains compound or trade off when techniques are stacked. Figure \ref{fig:object_combo_summary} examines this by summarising the effect of combining the main compression approaches. As with before, the results are averaged across all model sizes and both model families, providing a general sense of how the techniques interact when combined, while ignoring the effect of model size and family on compression efficiency.

\input{tables/object_combo_summary}

Figure \ref{fig:object_combo_summary} shows that hardware acceleration remains a clear first step, gaining between 20-40\% latency reduction for only ~2\% mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction, regardless of the technique combination. Note that the distillation latency gain here is largely mitigated, with Figure \ref{fig:object_combo_summary} indicating that it results in a similar latency, with just slight losses in accuracy. More aggressive combinations introduce increasingly pronounced trade-offs: distillation, pruning, and their combination all incur latency increases and mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions relative to the accelerated baseline, with INT8-based paths achieving the largest latency improvements at the greatest analytical cost. FP16-based combined paths occupy a more favourable middle ground, providing substantial latency reductions while preserving considerably more analytical performance than their INT8 counterparts.

The most notable result from this Figure is that the combination of hardware acceleratrion, FP16 quantisation, and 75\% input resolution achieves one of the stronger trade-offs on average. As mentioned before, this ignores the effect of model family and size on compression efficiency, and as such it is possible that large/small model behaviour is masking stronger trade-offs for specific model sizes. This will be explored in more detail in the per-technique analysis sections later, but this analysis is still worth noting. With a summary of the object detection compression results, the optimisation of pitch keypoint models can now be examined in the same way.
\subsubsection{Keypoint Detection Model Compression Summary}
Figure \ref{fig:pose_technique_summary} displays the same summary view for the pitch keypoint models, averaging the effect of each base technique across all paired model sizes and both YOLO11-pose and YOLO26-pose families.

\input{tables/pose_technique_summary}

The same trends observed for object detection holds here: hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation offer the most favourable accuracy--latency trade-offs, while more aggressive techniques incur steeper analytical costs. However, several important differences emerge when comparing the pose results to the object detection results. Note that acceleration produces \textbf{0.0\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ change for the pose models (rounded to one decimal place), an even cleaner result than for object detection (\textbf{-0.3\%}), while still delivering a strong \textbf{35.5\%} reduction in latency. FP16 quantisation similarly produces a negligible accuracy change (\textbf{-0.1\%}), suggesting that the keypoint models are effective at low-precisions.

By contrast, the pose models are considerably more sensitive to input resolution reduction and pruning than their object detection counterparts. Input resolution reduction at 60\% and 50\% caused mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions of \textbf{-28.2\%} and \textbf{-47.6\%} respectively, compared to \textbf{-9.1\%} and \textbf{-15.4\%} for object detection --- suggesting that accurate keypoint localisation is highly dependent on fine spatial detail that is lost at lower resolutions. Pruning similarly produces much larger accuracy penalties for pose models (\textbf{-24.5\%} to \textbf{-26.2\%}) compared to object detection (\textbf{-1.6\%} to \textbf{-4.6\%}), indicating that the keypoint task is considerably more sensitive to weight removal. INT8 quantisation also has an accuracy cost of \textbf{-43.8\%}, again far exceeding the object detection penalty of \textbf{-9.9\%}. As with before, the knowledge distillation data reflect the in-efficient PyTorch implementation artefact. With individual techniques analysed, the interaction of how these gains compound or trade off when stacked can now be examined. Figure \ref{fig:pose_combo_summary} summarises the combined compression paths for the pose models, again averaged across all model sizes and both pose model families.

\input{tables/pose_combo_summary}

Figure \ref{fig:pose_combo_summary} indicates that the combination of distillation and pruning performs remarkably well for pose models: Distill+Prune+Accelerated achieves only a \textbf{-0.1\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction with an \textbf{44.2\%} latency improvement, and Distill+Prune+FP16 achieves just \textbf{-0.3\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction with a \textbf{56.4\%} latency improvement. This is a notably stronger result than pruning alone, and suggests that distillation is effectively recovering the accuracy lost through pruning in the pose task. Distillation alone also appears beneficial for pose models, with Distilled+Accelerated producing a \textbf{+0.9\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ improvement alongside a \textbf{36.3\%} latency reduction.

As with object detection, INT8 and aggressive input resolution reduction produce the largest latency gains (\textbf{82.8\%} at 640px) but at severe accuracy costs (\textbf{-54.9\%}), making these paths unlikely to be viable for pitch keypoint estimation in most deployment scenarios. Hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation remain the most reliable starting points, and the Distill+Prune path emerges as a particularly promising avenue for pose models specifically.

With a broad summary of compression effects on both tasks, a more detailed per-technique analysis can now be carried out. As discussed earlier, this will allow the effect of model family and size on compression efficiency to be explored.

\subsection{Individual Compression Technique Analysis}
\subsubsection{Hardware Acceleration}

Figure \ref{fig:hardware_acceleration_results} presents the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency relationship for the uncompressed and FP32-accelerated YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose model families.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Hardware acceleration trade-off by architecture and task.}
    \label{fig:hardware_acceleration_results}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_baseline_pairs.png}
\end{figure}

Across almost all model families, sizes, and tasks, hardware acceleration produces a consistent leftward shift in the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$--latency plot with negligible vertical movement. This indicates substantial latency improvements with near-zero accuracy cost. This pattern holds across both object detection and pose estimation tasks, and across both YOLO11 and YOLO26 families, suggesting that the benefit of engine export is largely effective, regardless of scale.

The primary exception is the nano-scale models (YOLO11n, YOLO26n, and YOLO26n-pose), which display smaller latency improvements, alongside more noticeable mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ drops. This suggests that the smallest models are already operating near the memory and compute limits of the Jetson Orin Nano in their uncompressed form, leaving less headroom for the engine export to exploit. A secondary observation is that the larger models (L and X variants, shown in red and purple) tend to exhibit the largest absolute latency reductions upon acceleration, which is expected given that larger models have more redundant computation for the hardware optimiser to exploit. Overall, this suggests that hardware acceleration is a low-risk first compression technique to apply, as it delivers meaningful latency improvements with minimal accuracy cost across the majority of models.


\subsubsection{Input Resolution Reduction}

Figure \ref{fig:input_resolution_reduction_results} shows the effect of reducing the input resolution on the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency trade-off for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose models. Each line traces the progression of a single baseline model through increasingly aggressive resolution reduction (75\%, 60\%, and 50\% of the original input size).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Input resolution reduction trade-off.}
    \label{fig:input_resolution_reduction_results}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_input_resolution_panels.png}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models, resolution reduction produces a consistent and meaningful latency improvement across all model sizes, with the larger variants (L and X) achieving the greatest absolute reductions. Note that the loss in accuracy at the 75\% setting is small, and most models still achieve strong mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ values. However, the 60\% and 50\% settings produce increasingly steep accuracy degradations, and the trade-off curves begin to bend sharply downward, particularly for the smaller nano and small variants where the baseline accuracy is already lower. This suggests the obejct models cannot tolerate significant resolution reduction without substantial accuracy loss, likely due to the loss of  detail required to localise small objects such as the ball.

The keypoint analysis indiates that the YOLO11-pose variants suffer a moderate but manageable accuracy drop at 75\%, but the YOLO26-pose family degrades sharply across all three resolution levels (with several models falling to near-zero mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ at low input sizes). This supports the summary analysis that keypoint localisation is highly sensitive to input resolution, since the detail needed to predict joint positions is heavily affected by downsampling. Note that the main exception is YOLO26n-pose, which maintains a relatively favourable accuracy--latency trade-off across all resolutions, suggesting that this smallest variant may be more robust to reduced resolution.

Overall, input resolution reduction appears to be a viable compression strategy for object detection at moderate reduction levels, but should be applied with caution for pose estimation, where even modest resolution changes may produce significant accuracy losses.


\subsubsection{Quantisation}

Figure \ref{fig:quantisation_results} shows the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency trade-off for FP32, FP16, and INT8 quantisation across the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose families. As with input reduction, each line traces the progression of a single baseline model through the FP16 and INT8 quantisation stages to allow the effect on inference speed and accuracy retention to be examined across both model scale and task type.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Quantisation trade-off.}
    \label{fig:quantisation_results}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_quant_pairs.png}
\end{figure}

For all object detection model families and sizes, FP16 quantisation produces a consistent and meaningful leftward shift. This confirms that FP16 is a low-risk quantisation strategy for object detection, preserving the vast majority of analytical performance while delivering substantial latency reductions. The INT8 stage pushes latency lower still, but introduces a more visible downward shift in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$, particularly for smaller variants.

The pitch keypoint analysis also indicates that FP16 quantisation is effective across family and size (consistent with the object detection results). However, INT8 quantisation significantly lowers accuracy for many variants, especialy 11m-pose, 26m-pose, 11s-pose, and 26n-pose. This aligns with the input resolution analysis earlier, indicating that keypoint localisation depends on precise spatial information, which can be lost by INT8 quantisation.

Overall, FP16 quantisation emerges as a reliable compression step for both tasks, offering strong latency improvements with minimal accuracy cost. INT8 quantisation, by contrast, should be considered viable only for object detection at larger model scales, and should be avoided entirely for pose estimation given the severity of the observed accuracy degradation.

\subsubsection{Pruning}

Figure \ref{fig:pruning_results} illustrates the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ vs. latency relationship for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose models under increasing pruning strength. As with before, each line displays a baseline model through 90\%, 80\%, and 70\% weight retention prunings,. Note that YOLO26-pose pruning results are omitted from Figure \ref{fig:pruning_results} due to training instability encountered during pruning at this architecture and task combination.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Pruning trade-off.}
    \label{fig:pruning_results}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{images/map50_95_vs_latency_pruning_pairs_nsmlx.png}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models, pruning produces modest but consistent latency reductions across both YOLO11 and YOLO26 families, with the larger model variants exhibiting the most meaningful speed improvements. The mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ degradation is not significant at the 90\% retention level, particularly for the medium-to-large model scales, suggesting that light pruning is a possible secondary compression method after quantisation and input reduction. At the 80\% retention level (and 70\% if the pruning successed), accuracy degradation becomes more pronounced, particularly for the smaller variants, indicating that heavy pruning may not be suitable in this circumstance.

The pitch keypoint models show different trends; across almost all model variants, substantial mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions exist without significant latency improvements. This is consistent with the resolution reduction and INT8 quantisation observations, and further supports the conclusion that keypoint localisation is highly dependent on spatial details, and are not conductive to aggresive compression strategies.

\subsubsection{Knowledge Distillation}

Fianlly, Figure \ref{fig:knowledge_distillation_results} presents the effect of knowledge distillation on the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency trade-off for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose model families. To negate the PyTorch implementation in-efficiencies discussed in the summary analysis, all distilled models presented here have been exported to accelerated engine format prior to evaluation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Knowledge distillation trade-off.}
    \label{fig:knowledge_distillation_results}
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{images/map50_95_vs_latency_knowledge_ns_chains.png}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models (and especially smaller models), knowledge distillation produces only marginal changes in both latency and mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ relative to the accelerated baseline. This indicates that distillation, in isolation, offers limited utility as a standalone compression technique for object detection in this context, as the student models fail to achieve meaningful latency reductions.

The pose model results are more varied. The YOLO11-pose family exhibits a broadly similar pattern to the object detection models, with distilled variants remaining close to their baselines in both accuracy and latency. The YOLO26-pose family, however, displays considerably more instability: several distilled variants suffer notable mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions with little corresponding latency benefit, suggesting that the YOLO26-pose architecture is less compatible with knowledge transfer. Overall, knowledge distillation in isolation appears to offer limited standalone compression benefit in this study.

\subsection{Best Models}

With the compression techniques and their effectiveness analysed, the optimal object detection and pose estimation models can now be selected for the full-stack system. Given a target system frame rate of approximately 20 FPS, the combined latency budget for both models sits at around 40--50 ms. Accounting for system-level optimisations such as inference intervals and parallelisation, this budget can reasonably be extended to approximately 80 ms, allocating roughly 40 ms per model. Within this constraint, the models to be chosen are filtered to those remaining within budget while preserving as much of the baseline mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ as possible.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Best object detection models under 40 ms latency.}
    \label{fig:best_object_models}
    \includegraphics[width=.75\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/best_model_analysis_object.png}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:best_object_models} reveals a clear Pareto frontier among the candidate object detection models. At the higher end of the latency budget, the \textbf{FP16-quantised YOLO26s} model achieves the strongest mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$, with a distilled variant \textbf{(26s from 26m FP16)} offering a comparable trade-off at a marginally higher latency. At intermediate latencies, the \textbf{INT8-quantised YOLO26s} and the 960px input resolution variants provide competitive options, with pruned combinations also performing well in this range. At the lower end of the latency budget, the \textbf{FP16-quantised YOLO11n} and its distilled and pruned variants offer strong trade-offs, as does the \textbf{YOLO26s model at 60\% input resolution}. The project aims state that the compressed models should maintain within 15\% of the prior models in literature. As discussed in the literature review, strong mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ for player/ball detection models in the literature is approximately 0.93 \cite{roboflow_football_analytics_gnykk_model1}, and all models selected here achieve above $.93* .85=.795$, indicating that they meet this criterion.

Each model combination was evaluated on a continuous ten-minute football video, and the resulting pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ and end-to-end latency, GPU, power, and temperature.  The figure also also displays a green region, indicating a performance with-in 15\% of the baseline (as stated in the problem aims). While this falls short of the 20 FPS target, the result represents the pipeline operating without parallelisation or varied inference interval, both of which are likely to yield meaningful further reductions in effective latency. this represents a clear area for future improvement.

This pattern indicates that the shortlisting process has successfully identified models that extract strong analytical performance relative to their resource consumption, rather than simply those that happen to consume the most hardware resource.
