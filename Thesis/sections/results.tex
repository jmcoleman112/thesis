\section{Results}

With a literature review and methodology established, the results of the evaluation can be presented below. \textit{\textbf{Note:}} These immediate subsections largely focus on the results themselves, with interpretations and discussions explored later in the Discussions and Conclusions section (Section \ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Selecting Model Architecture and Dataset}
\label{lit:prelim_comparison}
As discussed earlier, preliminary experiments were first carried out to determine the model family and training dataset to be used for the majority of the study. These experiments compared possible object detection architectures in terms of analytical performance and latency, and also compared several labelled football broadcast datasets. Figure~\ref{fig:arch_compare} compares the analytical performance and latency of a range of object detection model families, while Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_compare} shows the preliminary dataset comparison, in which a range of YOLO11 model variants were trained and evaluated across the SoccerNet \cite{tracking}, Roboflow \cite{roboflow_sports_repo}, and Football Analytics \cite{roboflow_football_analytics_gnykk_model1} datasets. Note that in the plot below, Football Analytics is shortened to F.A. for concision.

As can be seen from Figure~\ref{fig:arch_compare}, although Faster R-CNN \cite{7485869} and SSD achieved slightly higher initial accuracy, the YOLO family \cite{UltralyticsYOLO} provided substantially lower latency on the Jetson Orin Nano. As a result, YOLO-based models were selected for the subsequent object detection experiments, as they offered the most suitable balance between accuracy and real-time edge deployment performance. Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_compare} indicates that the Football Analytics dataset achieved the strongest initial $\mathrm{mAP}_{50\text{--}95}$, at approximately \textbf{91\%} regardless of YOLO11 model size, and was therefore selected for the main study.



\subsection{Summary of Model Training}



With the broad model architecture and dataset selected, the full training and compression study was then carried out. In total, \textbf{904} unique model artefacts were generated and evaluated across the object detection and pitch keypoint tasks. This included 232 \textbf{YOLO11} models, 236 \textbf{YOLO11-pose} models, 204 \textbf{YOLO26} models, and 232 \textbf{YOLO26-pose} models. The majority of this consisted of compressed variants rather than baseline models, reflecting the first central aim of the study: to examine how different compression techniques affect model performance on the edge device. 

\subsection{Compression Analysis Summary}
Before individually exploring each compression technique and desired hardware resource reduction, it is useful to first summarise the overall trends across all trained and compressed models. The following subsections provide a high-level overview of how each compression technique (and combination of techniques) affected the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ and latency. These summaries allow the general patterns to be identified before a more detailed per-technique analysis is done.

\subsubsection{Compression Summaries: Object Models}
Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} below displays the average change in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ and latency for each of the main optimisation techniques applied to the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object detection models. To do so, individual model results are averaged across all sizes (N, S, M, L, X) and families (YOLO11 and YOLO26), providing a general sense of the effect of each technique. The effect of model size and family on compression efficiency that is ignored by this summary will be explored in more detail in the per-technique analysis sections later.

\input{tables/object_technique_summary}

As Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} shows, hardware acceleration is a highly effective technique, reducing latency by \textbf{31.0\%} with a negligible \textbf{-0.3\%} change in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$. FP16 quantisation is also valuable, offering a stronger latency improvement (\textbf{42.8\%}) with a small but slightly larger mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ cost (\textbf{-1.6\%}). INT8 lowers latency further (\textbf{52.6\%}), but at a much steeper average cost to accuracy (\textbf{-9.9\%}). Input resolution scaling presents a more incremental trade-off: the 75\% variant produces a strong latency reduction (\textbf{31.5\%}) with a reasonable accuracy cost (\textbf{-3.1\%}), while 60\% and 50\% exhibit increasingly steeper trade-offs. Pruning provides small latency gains at 90\% retention, with a more meaningful reduction only emerging at the most aggressive setting (\textbf{13.4\%} at 70\%). This high pruning comes with a non-negligible accuracy cost of \textbf{-4.6\%}. 

Note that while Figure \ref{fig:object_technique_summary} indicates that knowledge distillation appears to inflate latency in this summary, this is likely an artefact of an inefficient use of PyTorch to implement knowledge distillation. This does not materially affect the quality of analysis, as the true effect of knowledge distillation can instead be analysed later when the models are exported to hardware-specific \verb|.engine| formats.

With the results of the individual techniques established, it is now possible to examine how their effects interact when techniques are stacked. Figure \ref{fig:object_combo_summary} examines this by summarising the effect of combining the main compression approaches. As with before, the results are averaged across all model sizes and both model families, providing a general sense of how the techniques interact when combined, while temporarily ignoring the effects of model size and family.

\input{tables/object_combo_summary}

Figure \ref{fig:object_combo_summary} shows that hardware acceleration remains a clear first step regardless of secondary techniques, gaining between \textbf{20--40\%} latency reduction for only \textbf{\mbox{$\sim$2\%}} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction. Note that the inefficient distillation implementation mentioned earlier is largely mitigated here; for example, distilled and pruned combinations offer similar latencies to pruned combinations, but with increases in accuracy. More aggressive combinations introduce increasingly pronounced trade-offs: distillation, pruning, and their combination all incur similar latency reductions and mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions relative to the accelerated baseline, with INT8-based paths achieving the largest latency improvements at the greatest analytical cost. FP16-based combined paths occupy a more favourable middle ground, providing substantial latency reductions while preserving considerably more analytical performance than their INT8 counterparts.

The most notable result from this figure is that the combination of hardware acceleration, FP16 quantisation, and 75\% input resolution achieves one of the stronger trade-offs on average. As mentioned before, this ignores the effect of model family and size on compression efficiency, and as such it is possible that large or small-model behaviour is masking stronger trade-offs for specific model sizes. This will be explored in more detail in the per-technique analysis sections later, but this analysis is still worth noting. 

With a summary of the object detection compression results, the compression of pitch keypoint models can now be examined in the same way.
\subsubsection{Keypoint Detection Model Compression Summary}
Figure \ref{fig:pose_technique_summary} displays the same summary view for the pitch keypoint models, averaging the effect of each base technique across all paired model sizes and both families.

\input{tables/pose_technique_summary}

The same trends observed for object detection hold here: hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation offer the most favourable accuracy--latency trade-offs, while more aggressive techniques produce steeper analytical costs. However, several important differences emerge when comparing the keypoint results to the object detection results. The most notable is that these models seem considerably more sensitive to input resolution scaling and pruning than the object detection models. Input resolution reduction at 60\% and 50\% caused mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions of \textbf{-28.2\%} and \textbf{-47.6\%} respectively, compared to \textbf{-9.1\%} and \textbf{-15.4\%} for object detection. This suggests that accurate keypoint localisation is highly dependent on fine spatial detail that is lost at lower resolutions. Pruning similarly produces much larger accuracy penalties for pose models (\textbf{-24.5\%} to \textbf{-26.2\%}) compared to object detection (\textbf{-1.6\%} to \textbf{-4.6\%}), indicating that the keypoint task is considerably more sensitive to weight removal. INT8 quantisation also has an accuracy cost of \textbf{-43.8\%}, again far exceeding the object detection drop of \textbf{-9.9\%}. As with before, the knowledge distillation data reflect the inefficient PyTorch implementation artefact and will be analysed later. 

With individual techniques analysed for the keypoint models, the way the effects interact when combined can now be examined. Figure \ref{fig:pose_combo_summary} summarises the combined compression paths, again averaged across all model sizes and both pose model families.

\input{tables/pose_combo_summary}

Figure \ref{fig:pose_combo_summary} indicates that the combination of distillation and pruning performs remarkably well for keypoint models: Distill+Prune+Accelerated achieves only a \textbf{-0.2\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction with an \textbf{44.2\%} latency improvement, and Distill+Prune+FP16 achieves just \textbf{-0.3\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction with a \textbf{56.4\%} latency improvement. Distillation alone also appears beneficial for keypoint models, with Distilled+Accelerated producing a \textbf{-0.9\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ change alongside a \textbf{36.3\%} latency reduction.

As with object detection, INT8 and aggressive input resolution reduction produce the largest latency improvements (\textbf{82.8\%} at 640px) but at severe accuracy costs (\textbf{-54.9\%}), making these paths unlikely to be viable for pitch keypoint estimation in most deployment scenarios. Hardware acceleration and FP16 quantisation remain the most reliable starting points, and light distillation with light pruning may also be an effective strategy.


\subsection{Individual Compression Technique Analysis}

With a broad summary of compression effects on both tasks, a more detailed per-technique analysis can now be carried out. As discussed earlier, this will allow the effect of model family and size on compression efficiency to be explored.

\subsubsection{Hardware Acceleration}

Figure \ref{fig:hardware_acceleration_results} presents the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency relationship for the uncompressed and hardware accelerated YOLO11 and YOLO26 model families, separated by object and pitch keypoint tasks.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_baseline_pairs.png}
       \caption{Hardware Acceleration Trade-off Analysis.}
    \label{fig:hardware_acceleration_results}
\end{figure}

Across almost all model families, sizes, and tasks, hardware acceleration produces a consistent leftward shift in the plot with negligible vertical movement beyond \textbf{1-2\%}, which indicates substantial latency improvements with near-zero accuracy cost. This pattern holds across both object detection and keypoint estimation tasks, and across both YOLO11 and YOLO26 families, suggesting that the benefit of acceleration is substantial regardless of the specific model or task considered. A slight exception to this is the nano-scale models (YOLO11n, YOLO26n, and YOLO26n-pose), which display smaller latency improvements alongside more noticeable mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ drops. This suggests that the smallest models are already operating near the memory and compute limits of the Jetson Orin Nano in their uncompressed form, leaving less headroom for the engine export to exploit. A complementary observation is that the larger models (L and X variants, shown in red and purple) tend to exhibit the largest absolute latency reductions upon acceleration, which is expected given that larger models have more computations for the hardware optimiser to exploit. 

Overall, this suggests that hardware acceleration is a low-risk first compression technique to apply, as it delivers meaningful latency improvements with minimal accuracy cost across most models.


\subsubsection{Input Resolution Reduction}
\label{input_resolution_reduction_discussion}

Figure \ref{fig:input_resolution_reduction_results} shows the effect of reducing the input resolution on the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency trade-off for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose models. Each line traces the progression of a single baseline model through increasingly aggressive resolution reduction (75\%, 60\%, and 50\% of the original input size).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_input_resolution_panels.png}
        \caption{Input Resolution Reduction Trade-off Analysis.}
    \label{fig:input_resolution_reduction_results}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models, resolution reduction produces a consistent and meaningful latency improvement across all model sizes, with the larger variants (L and X) achieving the greatest absolute reductions. Note that the loss in accuracy at the 75\% setting is small, and most models still achieve mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ values within \textbf{5-8\%} of the baseline. However, the 60\% and 50\% settings produce increasingly steep accuracy degradations beyond \textbf{10\%} mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ losses, and the trade-off curves begin to bend sharply downward. This is particularly noticeable for the smaller nano and small variants where the baseline accuracy is already lower. This suggests the object models cannot tolerate significant resolution reduction without substantial accuracy loss, but can maintain performance at low to moderate reduction levels.

The keypoint analysis indicates that the YOLO11-pose variants suffer a moderate but manageable accuracy drops of under \textbf{10\%}, but the YOLO26-pose family degrades sharply across all three resolution levels (with several models falling to near-zero mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ at low input sizes). This supports the summary analysis that keypoint localisation is highly sensitive to input resolution, since the detail needed to predict keypoints is heavily affected by downsampling. Note that the main exception is YOLO26n-pose, which maintains a relatively favourable accuracy--latency trade-off across all resolutions, suggesting that this smallest variant may be more robust to reduced resolution.

Overall, input resolution reduction appears to be a viable compression strategy for object detection at moderate reduction levels, but should be applied with caution for pose estimation, where even modest resolution changes may produce significant accuracy losses.


\subsubsection{Quantisation}

Figure \ref{fig:quantisation_results} shows the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency trade-off for FP32, FP16, and INT8 quantisation across the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pose families. As with input reduction, each line traces the progression of a single baseline model through the FP16 and INT8 quantisation stages, allowing the effect on inference speed and accuracy retention to be examined across both model scale and task type.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/map50_95_vs_latency_quant_pairs.png}
        \caption{Quantisation Trade-off Analysis.}
    \label{fig:quantisation_results}
\end{figure}

For all object detection model families and sizes, FP16 quantisation produces a consistent and meaningful leftward shift with an mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ loss of less than \textbf{2\%}. This confirms that FP16 is a low-risk quantisation strategy for object detection, preserving the vast majority of analytical performance while delivering substantial latency reductions. The INT8 stage pushes latency lower still, but introduces a more visible downward shift in mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$, particularly for smaller variants.

The pitch keypoint analysis also indicates that FP16 quantisation is effective across model families and sizes, consistent with the object detection results. However, INT8 quantisation significantly lowers accuracy for many variants, especially YOLO11m-pose, YOLO26m-pose, YOLO11s-pose, and YOLO26n-pose. This aligns with the input resolution analysis earlier, indicating that keypoint localisation depends on precise spatial information, which can be lost through INT8 quantisation.

Overall, FP16 quantisation emerges as a reliable compression step for both tasks, offering strong latency improvements with minimal accuracy cost. INT8 quantisation, by contrast, should be considered viable only for object detection at larger model scales, and should be avoided almost always for keypoint localisation given the severity of the observed accuracy degradation.

\subsubsection{Pruning}

Figure \ref{fig:pruning_results} illustrates the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ vs. latency relationship for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pitch keypoint models under increasing pruning strength. As before, each line traces a baseline model through 90\%, 80\%, and 70\% weight-retention pruning levels. Note that YOLO26-pose pruning results are omitted from Figure \ref{fig:pruning_results} due to training instability encountered during the pruning of this specific architecture–task combination. This will be explored further in the Discussions section (specifically Section \ref{task_compression_discussion}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{images/map50_95_vs_latency_pruning_pairs_nsmlx.png}
        \caption{Pruning Trade-off Analysis.}
    \label{fig:pruning_results}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models, pruning produces modest but consistent latency reductions across both YOLO11 and YOLO26 families, with the larger model variants exhibiting the most meaningful speed improvements. The mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ degradation is not significant at the 90\% retention level, often around a \textbf{$\sim$1\%} reduction, suggesting that light pruning is a possible secondary compression method after quantisation and input reduction. At the 80\% retention level, and at 70\% where pruning succeeded, accuracy degradation becomes more pronounced at \textbf{$\sim$2--3\%}, particularly for the smaller YOLO11 variants, indicating that heavy pruning may not be suitable in this context.

The pitch keypoint models show different trends; across almost all model variants, substantial mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions of \textbf{$\sim$10--50\%} exist without significant latency improvements. This is consistent with the resolution reduction and INT8 quantisation observations, and further supports the conclusion that keypoint localisation is highly dependent on spatial detail and is not conducive to aggressive compression strategies.

\subsubsection{Knowledge Distillation}

Finally, Figure \ref{fig:knowledge_distillation_results} presents the effect of knowledge distillation on the mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$--latency trade-off for the YOLO11 and YOLO26 object and pitch keypoint model families. To remove the PyTorch implementation inefficiencies discussed in the summary analysis, all distilled models presented here were exported to accelerated \verb|.engine| format prior to evaluation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{images/map50_95_vs_latency_knowledge_ns_chains.png}
        \caption{Knowledge Distillation Trade-off Analysis.}
    \label{fig:knowledge_distillation_results}
\end{figure}

For the object detection models (and especially smaller models), knowledge distillation produces only marginal changes in both latency and mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ relative to the accelerated baseline. This indicates that distillation in isolation offers limited utility as a standalone compression technique for object detection in this context, as the student models fail to achieve meaningful latency reductions or accuracy improvements. 

The YOLO11-pose family exhibits a broadly similar pattern to the object detection models, with distilled variants remaining close to their baselines in both accuracy and latency. The YOLO26-pose family, however, displays considerably more instability: several distilled variants suffer notable mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reductions with little corresponding latency benefit, suggesting that the YOLO26-pose architecture is less compatible with knowledge transfer. This could be due to the increased complexity of the YOLO26 architecture, coupled with the spatial requirements of keypoint localisation. Overall, knowledge distillation in isolation appears to offer limited standalone compression benefit. \textit{\textbf{Note:}} Earlier results from the summary analysis indicate that distillation may be effective when combined with other techniques; this will be explored in the next subsection.

\subsection{Best Models}

With the compression techniques and their effectiveness analysed, the optimal object detection and keypoint localisation models can now be selected for the full-stack system. As discussed in the problem aims, compressed models should achieve mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ values within 15\% of comparable models reported in prior literature. The background review earlier identified strong mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ values for player/ball detection models of around 0.915 \cite{footandball}; applying the 15\% tolerance yields a lower limit of $0.915 \times 0.85 \approx 0.78$. Likewise, the problem aims stated a minimum full-stack latency of 100ms, giving a roughly 50ms ceiling for the object and keypoint models components individually. Allowing headroom for pre and post-processing gives a tighter maximum model latency of 40 ms. Figure~\ref{fig:best_object_models} presents an mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ versus latency plot of object models within this region, with the best models highlighted in orange and non-optimal models displayed in grey.

Figure \ref{fig:best_object_models} reveals a clear Pareto frontier among the candidate object detection models. At intermediate latencies, the \textbf{INT8 26s} and \textbf{FP16 11n (either Distilled or 90\% Pruned)} variants offer strong trade-offs of mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ and latency. At the lower end of the latency budget, the \textbf{FP16 26s at 60\%/50\% Input Reduction} variants, alongside the \textbf{FP16 26m at 50\% Input Reduction and Pruned to 90\%} model, offer extremely low latencies, coupled with a significant $\sim$15\% mAP$_{50\text{--}95}$ reduction compared to the best models shortlisted.

To illustrate the behaviour of the shortlisted candidates, Figure \ref{fig:confusion_matrix} presents the normalised confusion matrix for the \textbf{YOLO26s model at 60\% input resolution and FP16 }. The primary source of error is ball detection, where 13\% of ball instances are missed entirely, likely due to the ball's small size, fast motion, and frequent occlusion in broadcast footage. A secondary source of error is the misclassification of background entities as players or referees, although referee--background misclassification is unimportant given that the referee class is not a primary input to high-level tactical analysis.

With the best object detection models identified, the same selection criteria can be applied to the keypoint localisation models. The dominance of the YOLO26n-pose family here is consistent with the earlier finding (see Section \ref{input_resolution_reduction_discussion}) that this variant offers higher robustness to compression relative to the broader pose model inventory.

\subsection{Full-Stack System}

With the 9 best-performing object models and 4 best-performing keypoint models identified, the full-stack system can now be prepared and evaluated for both analytical accuracy and hardware efficiency. The system itself will first be briefly described, after which the full-stack results are presented and analysed.

\subsubsection{Full-Stack System Performance}

The models identified in Figures \ref{fig:best_object_models} and \ref{fig:best_pose_models} above were evaluated within the full-stack pipeline to assess higher-level statistical accuracy against the SoccerNet baseline system, which achieves a pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ of \textbf{71.2\%} \cite{tracking}. Each model combination was evaluated on a continuous ten-minute football video, and pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$, end-to-end latency, GPU usage, power draw, and temperature were recorded. This video contained the first 10 minutes of a professional English soccer match, contained over 300 pass events throughout the 10 minutes, and was chosen specifically to be outside the datasets used for model training. The analysis is limited to one video due to the lack of multiple fully annotated videos that did not overlap with the training data. Figure \ref{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency} below displays the best 15 model combinations, as well as a green region indicating performance within 15\% of the baseline, in line with the project aims. Note that the visualisation endpoint was not enabled for this evaluation, as it was not a focus of optimisation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency.png}
        \caption{Full-stack Pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ versus Latency Analysis.}
    \label{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:sweep_new_map1_5_vs_latency} shows that the strongest model combinations approach the SoccerNet baseline pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ of 71.2\%, with the best result reaching \textbf{69.7\%} mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ and two other combinations within \textbf{10\%} of the baseline. This is a strong result, demonstrating that the compressed models can recover near-baseline analytical performance alongside significant latency improvements. The top-performing combinations operated at end-to-end latencies of 80--110 ms, corresponding to approximately 9--12 FPS; while this falls short of the ideal 20 FPS target, it meets the minimum latency requirement of 100 ms as originally stated in the problem aims. The pipeline was also evaluated without parallelisation or varied inference intervals, both of which are likely to yield meaningful reductions in effective latency. This therefore indicates a clear area for future improvement. 

Figure \ref{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown} displays the latency breakdown for the full-stack system, using uncompressed and compressed models to demonstrate the impact of compression.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../Figures/produced_images/latency_breakdown_compare.png}
        \caption{Full-stack System Latency Breakdown.}
    \label{fig:piechart_latency_breakdown}

The compression pipeline reduces total system latency from \textbf{203.9} ms to \textbf{90.6} ms, a reduction of \textbf{55.6\%}. The keypoint estimation component achieves the greatest relative improvement, falling from \textbf{64.2\%} to \textbf{31.0\%} of total latency, while object detection increases from \textbf{31.1\%} to \textbf{47.7\%}. Note that, while object detection's absolute latency decreases, its relative share increases due to the proportionally larger reduction achieved by the keypoint component. The other processing steps and team assignment also increase in relative share, indicating those areas as additional potential focuses for future optimisation.

With end-to-end latency characterised, the relationship between pass mAP$_{1\text{--}5}$ and other system-level performance metrics can now be examined.
