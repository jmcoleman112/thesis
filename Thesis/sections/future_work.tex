\section{Suggestions for Future Work}

As noted in previous sections, several avenues for future work could further improve the performance and applicability of the proposed system. These include:

\begin{itemize}
\item investigating alternative computer vision model architectures and datasets,
\item exploring different full-stack system structures,
\item evaluating the approach on a wider range of edge devices, and
\item applying the methodology to other structurally similar sports.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Datasets and Model Architectures}
One further area for future work is the exploration of alternative datasets and computer vision model architectures. With regard to datasets, the preliminary experiments conducted in this thesis compared only three publicly available football broadcast datasets, with only Football Analytics \cite{roboflow_football_analytics_gnykk_model1} explored in depth. Future work could explore a broader range of datasets, or investigate the effect of combining multiple datasets to increase training set diversity and size. Custom dataset collection and annotation tailored specifically to the deployment environment could also yield improvements in model performance.

With regard to model architectures, this thesis utilised the YOLO family of models due to their well-established balance of speed and accuracy and their suitability for edge deployment. However, a number of alternative architectures could be explored to investigate whether improved performance or efficiency can be achieved. For object detection, transformer-based architectures such as RT-DETR \cite{Zhao2023RTDETR} could be investigated further, as despite exhibiting higher latency in preliminary experiments, architectural refinements or hardware-specific optimisations may reduce this gap. For keypoint localisation, dedicated pose estimation architectures such as HRNet \cite{sun2019deephighresolutionrepresentationlearning} could be explored, as they are specifically designed to maintain high-resolution feature representations throughout the network, which may yield more precise keypoint predictions than a general-purpose detection model.


\subsection{Full-Stack System Structure}
A second clear area for future work is the structure of the full-stack system. In this thesis, a relatively simple pipeline was chosen to demonstrate the core concepts while maintaining a manageable scope focussed on model compression techniques. Different system structures could be explored in future work, with one particularly interesting direction being the use of a third model dedicated to ball localisation. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:confusion_matrix}, ball localisation is the weakest-performing aspect of the current system, and a dedicated model could improve this component. This could in turn allow more aggressive compression of the existing pipeline while still improving overall system accuracy and efficiency.

More optimal methods for processing the model outputs could also be explored, as a number of parameters were fixed for simplicity. For example, possession radii and minimum pass distances could instead be selected dynamically based on model outputs or video-specific factors.

Finally, the use of non-model-based keypoint detection could be explored. This would likely reduce GPU usage significantly, but may lead to less consistent accuracy under variations in video quality and lighting.


\subsection{Varied Edge Device Evaluation}
A third area for future work is the evaluation of the system across a broader range of edge devices. This thesis utilised the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano \cite{JetsonOrinNano} as the target edge platform, as it represents a capable and commercially available embedded computing device. However, the performance characteristics of the system may vary significantly across different hardware platforms, and it would be valuable to evaluate the system on a wider range of devices. These could include other members of the NVIDIA Jetson family, such as the Jetson AGX Orin, which offers substantially greater computational resources, or the Jetson Nano, which represents a more constrained and lower-cost alternative. Beyond the Jetson family, evaluation on other edge platforms such as the Raspberry Pi or Coral Dev Board could provide insight into the generalisation of the compression techniques applied in this thesis. Such evaluations would allow for a more comprehensive understanding of the trade-offs between cost, power consumption, and inference performance across the edge computing landscape.

\subsection{Applications to Different Sports}
Finally, the methodology developed in this thesis could be extended to different sports that share a structurally similar setup to football. The core system components (object detection, keypoint localisation, and event recognition) are not football-specific, and could be applied to any sport involving a defined playing surface and trackable players and objects. Some immediately obvious suggestions include rugby, where pitch geometry and player tracking requirements are broadly analogous, as well as hockey and basketball, which similarly involve a bounded playing area with well-defined landmarks. American football could also be explored, given the availability of broadcast datasets and the structured nature of the playing field. Applying the system to different sports would serve both to validate the generalisation of the approach and to identify any sport-specific challenges, such as differences in object scale, player density, or camera positioning, that may require modifications to the system or model training strategy.
