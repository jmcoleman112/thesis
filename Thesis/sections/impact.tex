\section{Impact of Research}

The research presented in this thesis has the potential to deliver impact across both the sports technology industry and the wider field of embedded computer vision, in terms of both ease of technical access
and sustainability. Historically, advanced soccer analytics have been accessible primarily to professional clubs and broadcasters, due to the requirement of expensive equipment or cloud-based processing pipelines. While prior work has explored model compression and edge deployment in isolation, there has been limited investigation into how these techniques interact with real-time performance on constrained hardware within the context of sports analytics. By demonstrating that a full-stack analysis system can run effectively on a low-cost edge device such as the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano, this work lowers the barrier to entry for clubs, coaches, and analysts who are operating without access to enterprise-grade resources. The analysis of compression techniques in this niche also provides information on how to lower computational and resource costs, reducing consumption and environmental impact. 

\subsection{Potential Benefits and Pathways to Impact}

The first and most direct potential beneficiaries of this research are football clubs, coaches, and analysts at the amateur and semi-professional levels. These groups often lack access to the high-end analytics tools used by elite teams, but could benefit greatly from affordable systems that provide insights into player positioning, team formations, and tactical patterns. By enabling real-time analysis on embedded hardware, this work could allow smaller clubs to make data-driven decisions during matches and training sessions without needing expensive infrastructure or cloud services. 

Another potential beneficiary group includes sports technology companies and developers of computer vision systems for sports analytics, including those beyond just soccer. The findings on model compression techniques and hardware acceleration may inform the design of more efficient algorithms and systems, leading to products that are both accurate and accessible.

The final potential beneficiary group is the broader research community in computer vision and embedded systems. The detailed analysis of compression techniques and their impact on latency and accuracy can serve as a valuable reference for researchers working on similar problems in other domains, such as surveillance, robotics, or autonomous vehicles. The methodologies and findings could be applied to a wide range of applications where the task at hand is hyper-specific, and real-time performance on a resource-constrained device is critical.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations and Risk Mitigation}

While the potential benefits of this research are clear, it is also important to consider the ethical implications of deploying computer vision systems in sporting environments, as well as the training and compression processes. The most notable of these concerns is privacy, as a system capable of real-time player detection, tracking, and positional analysis introduces issues around monitoring of individuals, consent, data ownership, and the potential for misuse. This is particularly relevant in amateur and semi-professional contexts, where players may not have formally consented to being analysed, and where robust data governance frameworks are less likely to be in place compared to professional organisations. This ethical concern is mitigated by ensuring that any tracking data does not attempt to identify individuals by number or position or name, and instead focuses on aggregated team-level insights. 

A second ethical consideration concerns the potential for performance data to be used in ways that are harmful to players. Granular tracking data such as positioning, movement patterns, and involvement in play could be used to unfairly attack individual players, particularly at lower levels of the game where players participate recreationally. Care should be taken to ensure that any deployment of such systems is accompanied by transparent communication to all participants about how data is collected, stored, and used, and that appropriate anonymisation practices are followed where possible. This can also be mitigated by ensuring that the system focuses on team-level insights rather than individual player performance, and by providing clear guidelines for responsible use of the data generated by the system.

The third and final ethical consideration concerns the datasets used for training. If images or video of players are used without appropriate consent or licensing, this can raise important privacy and ethical concerns. In this work, the primary object detection datasets sourced from SoccerNet \cite{tracking}, Football Analytics \cite{roboflow_football_analytics_gnykk_model1}, and Roboflow \cite{roboflow} were compiled from footage drawn from the five major European football leagues, where such material is available for fair, non-commercial research use. As this project is academic in nature and not intended for commercial deployment, it remains within that scope. 
\subsection{Sustainability Considerations and Sustainable Development Goals}


This thesis's core aims of deploying computer vision on edge hardware rather than relying on cloud-based inference directly demonstrate the sustainability benefits. Cloud-based computer vision inference pipelines typically involve transmitting video data to remote data centres, where it is processed on high-powered GPU clusters that consume significant energy and contribute to carbon emissions. By contrast, the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano operates at a fraction of the power draw of a server-grade GPU, and the compression techniques explored in this thesis reduce that footprint further. Even if not at scale, the difference between a cloud-dependent and an edge-based deployment represents a meaningful reduction in energy consumption per analysed match.

Beyond the system itself, the compression methodology developed in this thesis contributes to a broader goal of sustainable AI development. The field of deep learning has faced scrutiny over the energy cost of training and deploying large models. This work demonstrates that smaller, compressed models can retain the vast majority of their analytical utility, providing a concrete example of how performance and efficiency need not be in opposition. This aligns directly with the principles of green AI and responsible machine learning, which advocate for minimising computational resource usage without sacrificing meaningful capability.

In terms of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals \cite{un_sdgs}, this research most directly supports \textbf{SDG 9: Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure}, as it involves developing efficient technological infrastructure (compressed computer vision on edge). It also contributes to \textbf{SDG 13: Climate Action}, as reducing the energy footprint of these computer vision systems contributes towards lowering the carbon use of digital infrastructure. Finally, increasing access to affordable sports analytics aligns with \textbf{SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities}, by making tools previously available only to well-resourced professional clubs accessible to amateur and semi-professional organisations with limited budgets.
