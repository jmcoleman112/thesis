\section{Lay Abstract}

This work examines whether modern computer vision methods for soccer analysis can be compressed to run directly on compact, low-power hardware, without relying on costly external infrastructure. Two core tasks were explored: detecting objects such as the players and the ball, and identifying the lines and markings on the pitch. Results show that hardware acceleration and reduced numerical precision (quantisation) delivered the largest speed improvements with little loss in detection quality for both model tasks. However, pitch keypoint detection proved considerably more unstable under the harsher techniques, degrading sharply when compression was applied aggressively. This suggests that precise localisation of field geometry is more compression-sensitive than detection of players and the ball.

The best models were integrated into a full system that estimates possession, passes, and generates formation maps from broadcast footage on a compact edge device. The strongest configurations indicate that useful, real-time soccer analytics is feasible on hardware compact enough for pitchside deployment.
