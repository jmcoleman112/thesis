\section{Lay Abstract}

This work examines whether modern computer vision methods for soccer analysis can be compressed to run directly on compact, low-power hardware, without relying on costly external infrastructure. Two core tasks were explored: detecting objects such as the players and the ball, and identifying the lines and markings on the pitch. Results show that hardware acceleration and reduced numerical precision (quantisation) delivered the largest speed improvements with little loss in detection quality for player and ball detection. Pitch keypoint detection proved considerably more unstable under the same techniques, degrading sharply when compression was applied aggressively. This suggests that identifying precise field geometry tolerates less compression than ball and player spotting.

The best models were integrated into a full system that estimates possession, passes, and generates formation maps from broadcast footage on a compact edge device. The strongest configurations indicate that useful, real-time soccer analytics is feasible on hardware compact enough for pitchside deployment.
