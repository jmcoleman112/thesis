\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
In line with the project aims and background literature review defined above, a methodology was created and followed, including specific tools and strategies that were utilised. This section will be focussed primarily on the training of initial models, the compression of these models, and the creation and optimisation of a full-stack system to utilise these models to apply high-level analysis of broadcast soccer film. 


\subsection{Model Training Procedure}
\label{subsec:training}

To support fair comparison, all uncompressed object detection and keypoint detection models were trained under a standard procedure. Training was initialised from COCO-pretrained weights \cite{lin2015microsoftcococommonobjects}, and then fine-tuned on a football-targeted dataset. All models were trained in PyTorch and saved as \verb|.pt| checkpoints prior to compression. Training used an input resolution of $1280 \times 1280$, a batch size of 32, and was conducted on the UCD high-performance cluster \textit{UCDSonic}. The $1280 \times 1280$ input resolution size was chosen as that was the default input size of most soccer analysis datasets. Optimisation used stochastic gradient descent with an initial learning rate of 0.01, momentum 0.937, and weight decay $5\times10^{-4}$ \cite{ultralytics_cfg}. Training ran for up to 500 epochs, with a 3-epoch warm-up schedule and early stopping patience of 60 epochs.

An initial benchmarking study was performed to identify the most suitable model family for the remainder of the work, based on analytical performance and hardware usage. A separate preliminary study was then conducted to determine the most suitable dataset for the full object detection study, in which 10 model variants were trained under identical conditions and evaluated across three labelled football broadcast datasets: SoccerNet \cite{tracking}, Roboflow \cite{roboflow_sports_repo}, and Football Analytics \cite{roboflow_football_analytics_gnykk_model1}. For pitch keypoint detection, the Roboflow football field dataset \cite{roboflow_football_field_detection_f07vi_dataset15} was used throughout.

\subsection{Hardware Acceleration}
To implement hardware acceleration without introducing other modifications, each trained PyTorch model was exported to a TensorRT \texttt{.engine} file in its native precision size (FP32) and input resolution (1280×1280 px). Although FP32 retains full numerical precision, the TensorRT export still applies graph-level optimisations including layer fusion, kernel selection, and improved memory scheduling, which reduce inference latency without altering model behaviour. This process is hardware specific and therefore must be performed on the target platform. This also limits the models portability to other hardware.

As the literature review indicated that hardware acceleration often offers significant improvement in hardware efficiency with no analytical loss, this method was applied to all PyTorch-based \verb|.pt| models when available. This includes uncompressed, pruned, distilled, and resolution-scaled variants, as well as all combinations of these techniques. 

\subsubsection{Quantisation}

Similar to hardware acceleration, FP16 and INT8 quantisation were implemented through post-training export of trained PyTorch checkpoints to TensorRT \texttt{.engine} files, but with reduced numerical precision applied to weights and activations. Post-training quantisation was used rather than quantisation-aware re-training for two reasons: it ensures a fair comparison by isolating the effect of precision reduction from any changes introduced during additional training, and it avoids the substantial computational cost that re-training the full model inventory at lower precision would have required. As with hardware acceleration, exports were performed at a fixed input resolution of 1280$\times$1280 px. For INT8 engine generation, a calibration dataset drawn from the training split was provided during export to estimate representative activation ranges. Without calibration, the quantised engine has no basis for determining how to map the full FP32 activation distribution into the narrower INT8 range, which can result in significant clipping and accuracy loss \cite{nvidia_modelopt}.

As with hardware acceleration, literature suggests that quantisation (and especially FP16 quantisation) can often be applied with minimal analytical loss and significant latency improvements, and so this method was applied to all PyTorch-based \verb|.pt| models when available, including all pruned, distilled, and resolution-scaled variants, as well as all combinations of these techniques.

\subsubsection{Input Resolution Scaling}
Input resolution scaling was also implemented through the post-training export process to TensorRT \texttt{.engine} files \cite{tensorrt}, maintaining the same native FP32 resolution but reducing the input size of the model. This was chosen rather than re-training models at different resolutions for the same reasons as with quantisation: fairness of comparison and to maintain a sustainable methodology. 

As model training was standardised at $1280 \times 1280$ px, input resolution scales of 75\%, 60\%, and 50\% were chosen. 50\% was selected as many computer vision models are trained at this resolution, and so it is a common default for real-time applications. 75\% and 60\% were then selected to provide a more gradual scaling curve. As mentioned in the literature review (Section \ref{lit:yolo}) that YOLO models require input dimensions to be multiples of 32 due to the stride of the detection head, and so the selected resolutions of $1280 \times 1280$ px, $960 \times 960$ px (75\%), $768 \times 768$ px (60\%), and $640 \times 640$ px (50\%).

\subsubsection{Pruning}
Pruning was implemented as structured channel pruning using NVIDIA ModelOpt FastNAS \cite{248452, nvidia_modelopt}. This approach searches for a reduced channel configuration under an explicit FLOP constraint, producing smaller and more efficient network variants while preserving a deployment-compatible architecture. ITrained YOLO checkpoints were pruned to FLOP retention levels of 90\%, 80\%, and 70\%. This range was selected to span a range of productive rentention levels indicated by literature \cite{10204354}. Following pruning, fine-tune training was applied under the same procedure as the uncompressed models but limited to 100 epochs, which was sufficient to recover most of the accuracy lost during pruning while remaining computationally sustainable and time-reasonable across the full model inventory. Structured pruning was used in preference to unstructured weight pruning as it preserves dense tensor operations and standard layer structures, which are more readily accelerated by inference frameworks such as TensorRT \cite{nvidia_modelopt}. Note that pruning could not be applied to all model variants, as aggressive channel removal in certain architecture and task combinations caused training instability during fine-tuning, likely due to the removal of feature channels critical to localisation.

\subsubsection{Knowledge Distillation}
Knowledge distillation was implemented as a teacher--student training strategy, in which a smaller YOLO student model was trained using both the standard detection loss and a CWD distillation loss computed against the intermediate feature maps of a larger, fixed teacher model \cite{Kaleem2024ACR}. Lightweight alignment layers were used where teacher and student feature dimensions differed. The distillation loss was weighted by a decaying schedule $\lambda_t$, placing greater emphasis on teacher guidance early in training before shifting focus toward the student's own detection objective.

Channel-Wise Distillation (CWD) was used as the feature-level distillation loss \cite{Kaleem2024ACR, make5040083}, which encourages the student to match the teacher's channel-wise spatial response patterns at each paired feature stage, helping the student learn more discriminative internal representations than it would from ground-truth labels alone.

For results analysis, teacher--student pairings were defined by the gap in scale between the two models: a small jump denotes one scale step between student and teacher (e.g. N$\rightarrow$S), a medium jump denotes two steps (e.g. N$\rightarrow$M), and a large jump denotes three or more steps (e.g. N$\rightarrow$L or beyond).

For this work, the MAXN uncapped power limit mode (shaded in yellow) was selected as it allows the GPU, CPU, and external memory controller (EMC) to operate at their maximum clock speeds, which would maximise throughput. \subsubsection{Analytical Performance Metrics}

For both object detection and keypoint localisation, model accuracy was evaluated using the $\mathrm{mAP}_{50\text{--}95}$ metric on a held-out validation subset \cite{Everingham2010}. This metric averages Average Precision (AP) across ten IoU thresholds from 0.50 to 0.95 in increments of 0.05, making it a stricter and more comprehensive measure than evaluating at a single threshold such as $\mathrm{mAP}_{50}$.

For object detection, the threshold at each step is Intersection over Union (IoU), which quantifies the spatial overlap between a predicted bounding box and its ground truth. For keypoint localisation, IoU is replaced by Object Keypoint Similarity (OKS), which evaluates predicted keypoint accuracy by computing the Euclidean distance between each predicted and ground truth keypoint, normalised by the scale of the object. Higher OKS thresholds penalise even small deviations in predicted keypoint positions, making $\mathrm{mAP}_{50\text{--}95}$ particularly demanding for precise localisation tasks \cite{Everingham2010}.

For full-stack temporal analysis, the metric $\mathrm{mAP}_{1\text{--}5}$ was used for pass detection, where predictions were matched to ground-truth pass events using temporal tolerances of 1 to 5 seconds rather than spatial thresholds, allowing performance to be assessed under increasing levels of timing leniency.

\subsubsection{Hardware Benchmarking Protocol}

Hardware usage was assessed using GPU utilisation, power draw, and device temperature, chosen for their relevance to the Jetson Orin Nano's constraints of an 85$^\circ$C thermal limit, a 25\,W power budget, and a 1024-core CUDA GPU \cite{JetsonOrinNano}. All telemetry was sampled every 200\,ms using NVIDIA's \verb|tegrastats| command, which internally averages over the preceding 50\,ms at each poll.

Benchmarking was performed in two complementary formats, each targetting a distinct purpose. For full-stack evaluation, a continuous soccer match video was processed for 600\,s with the complete pipeline active (object detection, keypoint estimation, and post-inference processing).  Note that the visualisation endpoint was disabled to avoid overhead due to video rendering. This format provides a realistic assessment of end-to-end system performance under representative operating conditions, but cannot isolate the contribution of individual model variants from the surrounding pipeline. For individual model evaluation, a fixed image set was processed in a continuous inference loop for 240\,s with five warm-up inferences discarded, ensuring the device remained under sustained load throughout the sampling window. This format enables direct comparison of hardware usage and latency across model variants in isolation, without overhead introduced by the fullstack system.


\subsection{Post-Processing and Higher-level Statistic Generation}
\label{subsec:postprocessing}

Following model inference, a post-inference pipeline was applied to generate higher-level football analytics. Through this pipeline, object identities, pitch-referenced player locations, team assignments, ball possession, pass events, and player speed estimates could be derived from the raw detector outputs.

\subsubsection{Multi-object Tracking}

Object detections for players, goalkeepers, referees, and the ball were maintained across frames using the ByteTrack multiple-object tracking (MOT) algorithm \cite{zhang2022bytetrack}. This allowed the system to assign a persistent identity to each detected object over time. ByteTrack was particularly suitable for this task because it does not rely only on high-confidence detections, and instead attempts to recover any unmatched tracks using lower-confidence detections. This helps maintain track continuity during short occlusions, missed detections, or brief drops in detector confidence, which are common in soccer footage due to player overlap, rapid motion, and small object size. A simplified version of the ByteTrack association process is outlined in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bytetrack} below.

  
\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Simplified ByteTrack association process \cite{zhang2022bytetrack}}
\label{alg:bytetrack}

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require tracks, detections
\State pred\_tracks = predict\_tracks(tracks)
\State high\_detects, low\_detects = filter\_detections(detections)

\Statex
\State matched\_high, unmatched\_tracks, unmatched\_high = link(pred\_tracks, high\_detects)
\State update\_tracks(matched\_high)
\Statex
\State matched\_low, unmatched\_tracks, unmatched\_low=link(unmatched\_tracks, low\_detects)
\State update\_tracks(matched\_low)
\Statex
\For{detection in unmatched\_high}
    \State create\_new\_track(detection)
\EndFor
\Statex
\For{track in unmatched\_tracks}
    \State mark\_as\_lost(track)
\EndFor
\Statex
\State remove\_expired\_tracks(tracks)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Homography Estimation}

To map detected objects from image coordinates to real-world pitch coordinates, the pitch keypoint model outputs were matched to a predefined top-down pitch template based on standard FIFA pitch dimensions. A homography matrix mapping image coordinates to canonical pitch coordinates was then estimated using RANSAC \cite{ransac}, providing robustness to missing or incorrectly localised keypoints. The estimated homography was smoothed over time to reduce noise from failed or low-confidence inferences, with the previous valid estimate retained when RANSAC failed to find a stable solution. For player and goalkeeper detections, the bottom-centre point of the bounding box was projected, as this most closely approximates foot placement on the playing surface. For the ball, the bounding-box centre was used.

\subsubsection{Player and Goalkeeper Team Assignment}

Team identity was determined from player appearance using K-means clustering analysis \cite{ZhengLiu2012ColorRecognition}. For each tracked player or goalkeeper, an upper-body crop of their bounding box was extracted and pitch-like green pixels were suppressed before colour estimation was performed. The dominant jersey colour was then estimated using a two-cluster K-means procedure \cite{ZhengLiu2012ColorRecognition}. The extracted colour for each track was temporally smoothed across frames to avoid noise from blurring, occlusion, and lighting variations. The smoothed colour representation is then compared against manually specified team colours (with separate colour references for outfield players and goalkeepers). This allows a per-player team identity estimate to be maintained across long-form video sequences.

\subsubsection{Possession, Pass, and Speed Estimation}

Ball possession was estimated using the projected top-down coordinates of the ball and nearby players. At each frame, possession was assigned to the nearest valid player or goalkeeper within a predefined control radius, with smoothing across frames and thresholds used to reduce possession flickering. Frame-level possession assignments were accumulated over time to compute team possession percentages.

Passes could then be recorded when possession changed from one player to a different player on the same team, with smoothing and thresholds again applied to reduce false positives from occlusion. If possession instead changed across teams, the event was treated as the beginning of a new possession sequence rather than a pass.

Player speed was estimated from consecutive 2D pitch positions. For each player, distance moved between frames was converted from pitch pixels to metres and smoothed to give distance travelled and speed.
